Appendix C — GPS Maturity Assessment Tool
A self-assessment instrument for locating institutional capability across all sixteen GPS categories and producing a prioritized development roadmap

How to use this tool

Who should participate

This assessment works best with a mixed group — executive leadership, board members, and frontline staff — because each group has different visibility into different aspects of institutional performance. Senior leadership tends to overestimate governance quality. Frontline staff tend to have more accurate assessments of operational process quality. Board members often have limited visibility into operational realities. Divergences between these groups are themselves diagnostic.

Recommended minimum group: executive director + 2 board members + 2 frontline staff. Optimal group: 8–12 participants representing all role levels and community member perspectives.

How long it takes

  • Full 16-category assessment: 2.5–3 hours with brief breaks
  • Priority-category assessment (6–8 categories): 75–90 minutes
  • Single-category deep dive: 15–20 minutes
  • Roadmap development session: 45–60 minutes after scoring

What to do with results

  • Complete the scoring summary (Section 3) immediately after assessment
  • Identify 2–3 priority development categories before leaving the session
  • Complete the development roadmap (Section 4) within two weeks
  • Present findings to governance body within one governance cycle
  • Reassess annually using the same instrument
The most important instruction: Assess where the institution actually is, not where leadership wants it to be or intends to get to. The assessment has no external audience. The only failure mode is self-deception that produces a development roadmap based on aspirational rather than actual capability. For each score, you must record specific evidence — a named document, a specific decision, a particular staff practice. "We believe we are at Level 3" is insufficient. "Our evidence is: our six-domain equity audit from March 2024 and the improvement plan adopted at the June board meeting" is the standard required.
Section 1 — Level legend: what each maturity level means
Level 1
Absent or informal
This process does not exist in the institution, or exists only in the informal behaviors of specific individuals — not as a designed institutional practice. If those individuals leave, the practice disappears. Governance body is unaware of performance in this domain or receives no systematic information about it.
Level 2
Defined and developing
The process has been formally designed and is beginning to be implemented. Written policies or procedures exist. Practice is inconsistent — some staff or governance members follow them, others do not. Data is beginning to be collected but is not yet driving decisions. Evidence of intent more than consistent execution.
Level 3
Consistent and managed
The process is consistently practiced across the institution. Written procedures are followed reliably. Data is collected, reviewed at governance level, and drives improvement decisions. The process survives leadership transitions. Most practitioners can describe the process and its purpose without prompting.
Level 4
Optimizing and contributing
The process is continuously improving based on data and reflection. The institution is recognized by peers as a resource or model in this domain. Improvements in this category are contributing to improvements in other GPS categories. Community members are active participants in shaping the process rather than subjects of it.

Tier I · Civic operating processes · GPS 1.0–6.0
GPS 1.0 Collective Vision & Governance Strategy Mission, governance bodies, decision rights, stakeholder engagement, governance health
1Absent / informal
  • Mission statement exists but was not developed with community participation
  • No decision rights map; community members cannot describe what authority they hold
  • No governance self-assessment process
  • Executive director reviewed informally or not at all
  • Succession plan absent for all critical roles
2Defined / developing
  • Mission developed with some community input; theory of change documented
  • Governance body composition somewhat documented; election processes exist
  • Decision rights partially mapped; some community members aware of their authority
  • Annual executive director review conducted; primarily activity-based
  • Some succession awareness; no formal succession plans
3Consistent / managed
  • Mission reviewed and updated through participatory process on regular cycle
  • Decision rights map published and communicated to all community members
  • Annual governance self-assessment conducted; findings drive development priorities
  • Executive director assessed against institutional commitments, not only activities
  • Succession plans current for ED and critical board roles
4Optimizing / contributing
  • Mission operationalization is measurable; values-alignment assessment drives governance decisions
  • Community members routinely exercise decision rights; at least 40% of major decisions traceable to constituent input
  • Governance model contributed to peer institutions; recognized as governance resource
  • Governance structures reformed in response to self-assessment findings
Assessment questions — require specific examples, not general tendencies
1.When was the decision rights map last published and communicated to community members? Can you name a specific community member who knows what decisions they have authority over?
2.Describe the last governance body self-assessment — when was it conducted, who participated, and what specific change did it produce?
3.When was the executive director last assessed against institutional commitments (not only financial and programmatic metrics)? What happened as a result?
4.Name one major institutional decision made in the past 12 months that was changed because of community member input. What was the mechanism?

Section 5 — Reassessment guidance

When and how to reassess

Annual reassessment is the standard recommendation. The reassessment should be conducted at the same time each year to enable meaningful year-over-year comparison. The same instrument should be used each time — do not modify the rubrics between assessments, as changes in wording will make it impossible to distinguish genuine maturity improvement from assessment drift.

Triggered reassessment — outside the annual cycle — is appropriate when: the institution undergoes major leadership transition (executive director departure, board chair departure, or replacement of more than 30% of the governance body); the institution makes a significant strategic pivot; the institution merges with or acquires another organization; or a major external event (funding crisis, pandemic, natural disaster) significantly disrupts institutional operations for more than three months.

The evidence standard for claiming improvement. Any score increase at reassessment requires specific evidence. "We have improved our GPS 11.0 score from 1.5 to 2.5" requires naming what specifically changed: a new process was implemented, a specific document was created, a specific governance decision was made, a specific staff practice changed. Claiming improvement without naming evidence is the assessment equivalent of claiming values alignment without operationalizing the values. The question "what specifically changed?" should be asked for every score that increases at reassessment.

What counts as maturity improvement. Moving from Level 1 to Level 2 in a category typically requires 6–18 months of sustained development work. Moving from Level 2 to Level 3 typically requires 12–24 months. Level 4 requires sustained excellence over multiple years and is unlikely to be achieved in fewer than 3–5 years from Level 1. Claims of rapid improvement across many categories simultaneously should be viewed with skepticism — the most common cause is score inflation at the initial assessment or at reassessment, not rapid genuine improvement.

Sharing assessment results. The GPS maturity profile is an internal governance document. Institutions may choose to share their profiles with peer institutions, funders, or community members — doing so requires accepting the accountability that comes with transparency about current weaknesses as well as strengths. Institutions that share only results that reflect well on them are not practicing the transparency that many of the GPS categories themselves require. The most honest and most useful sharing is of the full profile including the development roadmap — because it demonstrates not only where the institution is but what it is committed to doing about the gaps.

End of Appendix C — GPS Maturity Assessment Tool
Connection to Appendix B (KPI Library): The maturity assessment and the KPI library are complementary tools. The maturity assessment locates the institution on the capability development spectrum for each GPS category; the KPI library provides the specific measurements that track performance within each category. Institutions should complete a maturity assessment before selecting their active KPI set — the assessment identifies which categories require the most development investment and therefore which KPIs will be most valuable to track closely.

Connection to Chapter 24 (Five Archetypes): The maturity assessment is most useful when interpreted through the lens of the institution's archetype. A community land trust scoring Level 1 on GPS 10.0 is in a different governance situation than a mutual aid network scoring Level 1 on GPS 10.0 — the former has an urgent critical gap; the latter may not need GPS 10.0 above Level 2 for years. The archetype profiles in Chapter 24 provide the context for interpreting which GPS category gaps are urgent and which can be developed more slowly.
