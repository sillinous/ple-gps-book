Using the maturity model — where to start and how to improve

A practical guide to assessing where your institution is, interpreting what the gaps mean, and building a prioritized path forward without being overwhelmed by what is not yet built

Here is what actually happens when a leadership team sits down to assess their institution against the GPS framework. Someone looks at the list of 16 categories and 80 process groups and says: "We're not doing most of this." Someone else says: "We're doing some of it — just not deliberately." A third person, usually the one who's been there longest, says: "We've been doing that for years. We just don't call it anything." All three are right. The maturity model is the tool that makes sense of all three observations at once — that locates an institution honestly in each category, that explains why "we do something" and "we do it well" are different answers, and that turns the overwhelming length of the full framework into a manageable development sequence.

The maturity model is not a grading system. It does not sort institutions into good and bad. Two institutions can both score Level 1 on Category 11.0 (institutional integrity) and face completely different risk profiles depending on their size, governance history, and current growth trajectory. What the model does is locate — it places an institution on a developmental map so that the next right step becomes legible. Without that map, every category feels equally urgent, which means nothing gets prioritized, which means nothing gets built. With it, the choices become concrete: this first, then that, because of this dependency.

Read this chapter before diving into the category chapters in Parts III, IV, and V. Each of those chapters includes maturity indicators at all four levels, and they read differently once you've internalized what the levels actually mean versus memorized their labels.

The four maturity levels

The model uses four levels. The progression moves through four qualitatively different relationships an institution can have with any process domain: absence, definition, consistency, and improvement. These are not arbitrary steps on a spectrum — each represents a real threshold. The gap between Level 1 and Level 2 is whether anyone has decided to treat this as a process. The gap between Level 2 and Level 3 is whether the process works when the person who designed it is absent. The gap between Level 3 and Level 4 is whether the process gets better on its own, through deliberate learning, rather than requiring someone to periodically fix it. Those are three very different organizational challenges.

The four maturity levels — defined
1
Absent or informal
The process either does not exist in the institution, or exists only as ad hoc individual behavior with no organizational form, documentation, or repeatable structure. Outcomes depend entirely on who happens to be present and what they happen to know. When key people leave, the capability disappears with them.
No documented process or designated process owner
Outcomes are inconsistent and depend on individual knowledge
The institution cannot describe how it does this if asked
Staff departures in this area cause significant capability loss
2
Defined and developing
The process exists as a recognized institutional function with some documentation and at least informal ownership. It produces consistent outputs most of the time, but execution depends significantly on individual knowledge and motivation rather than reliable process design. The process is acknowledged as important and is being actively developed, but has not yet achieved the consistency required for reliable operation across different staff and conditions.
Process is named, acknowledged, and has a designated owner
Some documentation exists; gaps are known and being addressed
Outputs are consistent under normal conditions but degrade under stress
New staff can be onboarded to the process within a reasonable timeframe
3
Consistent and managed
The process operates reliably across different staff, conditions, and time periods. It is documented, owned, and executed consistently. Performance is measured, and the measures are used to manage execution. The process can survive the departure of its primary owner without significant disruption. This is the level that most institutional processes should aim for as their target — the point at which the process functions as a genuine institutional capability rather than a heroic individual effort.
Process documentation is complete, current, and actively used
Performance is measured against defined standards and reviewed regularly
Execution is consistent across staff and withstands personnel turnover
Deviations from standard are identified and addressed systematically
4
Optimizing and learning
The process not only performs consistently but continuously improves. The institution uses data from process execution to identify improvement opportunities, experiments with process changes, measures the results of those experiments, and incorporates learnings systematically. The process is a site of organizational learning, not just organizational execution. Maturity Level 4 is aspirational for most processes in most institutions — achieving it across all 16 categories is not a realistic near-term goal for any institution, and should not be treated as a benchmark against which current institutions are found wanting.
Process improvement is a regular, documented activity rather than a crisis response
Performance data drives continuous experimentation and adaptation
Process knowledge is actively contributed to the broader institutional community
The institution can teach this process to other institutions effectively
Conducting the assessment — five diagnostic questions

The assessment applies five diagnostic questions to each of the 16 categories. Answer them honestly about your institution — not aspirationally, not based on your best month — and you'll get a score that's actually useful. Each question corresponds to a maturity threshold. A "yes" to the first means you've cleared Level 1. "Yes" to the second means Level 2. The score for a category is the highest level where you can answer all questions affirmatively. Note that: the questions draw on knowledge that already exists inside the institution. No external audit required. The hard part isn't gathering information. It's being willing to answer accurately when the answer is worse than you'd like.

Five diagnostic questions — applied to each of the 16 categories
Q1
Does this process exist in your institution in any form?
Not necessarily well-designed or documented — just: does anyone do this, in any way, reliably enough that it could be called a practice?
1
No — process is absent or purely ad hoc
2+
Yes — some version of this exists and is recognized
Q2
Is the process documented and owned?
Can someone point to written documentation? Is there a named person or role responsible for its execution? Can a new person be onboarded to this process without relying entirely on the current owner's memory?
1
No documentation, no clear owner
2+
Some documentation exists; ownership is defined
Q3
Does it execute consistently across people and conditions?
Would the process produce similar outputs if a different person ran it? Does it hold up under pressure — when the primary owner is absent, when the institution is under stress, when circumstances are unusual?
1
Highly person-dependent; degrades under stress
2
Mostly consistent but some person-dependency remains
3+
Consistently executed regardless of who runs it
Q4
Is performance measured and actively managed?
Are there KPIs or measures for this process that are reviewed regularly? Does someone look at the results and make decisions based on them? Does poor performance trigger a response?
1–2
No measurement, or measurement that is not acted on
3+
Measured, reviewed, and used to manage execution
Q5
Does the process improve systematically over time?
Is there a regular practice of reviewing how the process is working and making deliberate changes to improve it? Are process improvements documented and shared? Does the institution contribute what it has learned to other institutions?
1–3
Process changes are reactive rather than deliberate
4
Continuous, documented, systematic improvement practice

This is what most mutual aid networks look like after an honest assessment. The civic operating categories score relatively well — especially those closest to direct service (4.0, 6.0) and community connection (3.0). That's the founding energy: show up, deliver, connect. Passion translates directly into these scores. The institutional support categories are weak across the board, with multiple Level 1s. The novel categories are essentially absent. This is the normal starting point for organizations built from community relationships rather than operational design. Nothing here is a moral failure. It is a development map.

The profile predicts specific failure modes based on the dependency relationships described in Chapter 6. Level 1 in Category 7.0 (human development) alongside Level 3 in service delivery means one thing: the Level 3 score is being sustained by individual people who haven't burned out yet. When they do — and they will — the service delivery score falls with them. Level 1 in Category 11.0 (integrity) means that as the organization grows and governance gets complicated, nothing is in place to prevent power from concentrating in the people who've been around longest. The novel category gaps are expected and not yet urgent. But they mark the work that's coming.

Prioritizing development — the four-quadrant model

A maturity profile almost always surfaces more development needs than any institution can address simultaneously. That's the expected outcome, not a problem with the assessment. Prioritization requires two inputs: the maturity gap (how far below optimal is this category?) and the impact weight (how consequential is this gap for the institution's specific mission, right now?). These two dimensions generate the four-quadrant model below. The quadrant isn't a formula — it's a forcing mechanism. It makes institutions commit to a priority order rather than treating everything as equally important, which is the same as treating nothing as important.

← Low impact
High impact →
Low gap · Low impact
Monitor and maintain
Categories that are already reasonably developed and not critical to current mission delivery. Maintain current level; review periodically. Do not invest development resources here when higher-priority gaps exist.
Low gap · High impact
Protect and strengthen
Categories that are well-developed and highly consequential — the institution's genuine strengths. Protect these from resource pressure; invest in moving them from Level 3 to Level 4 where possible. These are the capabilities that give the institution its identity.
High gap · Low impact
Develop when capacity allows
Underdeveloped categories that are not currently blocking mission delivery. Important to develop eventually — gaps here become consequential as the institution grows — but not the first call on scarce development resources.
High gap · High impact
Develop immediately
The highest-priority development investments: categories that are significantly underdeveloped and whose underdevelopment is creating real risk to mission delivery, institutional stability, or equity. This quadrant should receive the majority of development resources until the most critical gaps reach Level 2 or 3.

Which quadrant a category belongs to requires judgment the model can't make for you. Two institutions with identical maturity profiles can have entirely different priorities because their missions, scales, and current vulnerabilities differ. A 500-member cooperative federation faces different Category 11.0 (integrity) risks than a five-person collective — even if both score Level 1. The profile provides the gap dimension. The institution's leaders and members provide the impact weighting. Neither gets to skip their part.

One specific priority rule that applies across almost all institutional contexts: any category with a Level 1 score that sits in the direct dependency chain of a Level 3 or higher civic operating category should be treated as high impact regardless of what the institution's leaders believe about its urgency. The dependency relationships documented in Chapter 6 are empirical patterns from organizational practice, not theoretical constructs. An institution that scores Level 3 in Category 6.0 (member support) but Level 1 in Category 7.0 (human development) is operating on borrowed time. The dependency will resolve itself — through burnout, capability loss, or governance crisis — whether or not the institution acknowledges it.

The group assessment workshop

The most valuable use of the maturity assessment is not individual — it is collective. Running the assessment as a workshop with the institution's leadership team, governance body, or a representative cross-section of staff and members produces insights that no individual assessment might generate, because the disagreements that emerge in scoring are often more diagnostically valuable than the scores themselves.

When five people assess the same category and produce scores of 1, 2, 2, 3, and 3, the spread is not noise. It is signal. It typically indicates one of three things: the process is inconsistently executed across different parts of the institution; different people have different definitions of what "consistent execution" means; or some people have access to information about the process's actual performance that others do not. Any of these is important. None is visible in the individual assessment.

Group assessment workshop — recommended protocol
  • 01
    Individual scoring — silent and independent
    Each participant receives the assessment rubric and scores all 16 categories independently, without discussion. Silent scoring prevents anchoring bias — the tendency to defer to the first confident voice in the room. Allow 20–30 minutes. Scores are recorded privately before any discussion.
  • 02
    Score reveal and spread identification
    All scores are revealed simultaneously — on cards, sticky notes, or a shared digital tool. For each category, record the spread (minimum score, maximum score, median). Categories with high spread (range of 2 or more) are flagged for discussion. Categories where all scorers agree are noted and set aside.
  • 03
    Discuss high-spread categories first
    Start with the categories that produced the widest disagreement. Ask both the highest and lowest scorers to describe the specific evidence they used for their score. The goal is not to reach consensus but to understand why informed people see the institution differently. Often the explanation reveals a genuine capability gap that neither the high-scorers nor the low-scorers had fully articulated.
  • 04
    Agree on a working profile — not a final verdict
    After discussion, agree on a working score for each category that reflects the group's collective best judgment. Emphasize "working" — the profile is a starting point for development planning, not a permanent institutional verdict. Revisit it annually or after significant operational changes. Record the rationale for each score, not just the number.
  • 05
    Apply the prioritization framework collectively
    With the working profile complete, apply the four-quadrant prioritization model as a group. Ask: which categories are currently most consequential to our mission delivery? Which Level 1 scores are creating active risk right now? Which Level 3 scores are most at risk of degrading? Produce a ranked development agenda — the two or three categories the institution will invest in developing over the next year.
  • 06
    Assign development ownership and resources
    For each priority development category, assign a named owner, a six-month development goal expressed as a specific maturity transition (e.g., "Category 7.0 from Level 1 to Level 2"), a resource allocation (time, budget, or external support), and a review checkpoint. Development priorities without owners and resources are wishes, not plans.
Five common patterns — and what they mean

After applying the maturity model across many institutions of different types and stages, five recurring patterns appear with enough regularity that they deserve explicit naming. Each pattern is a diagnostic signal — a characteristic shape of institutional development that predicts specific future vulnerabilities and suggests specific development priorities. Recognizing the pattern your institution fits is the fastest way to identify which development investments matter most.

Five common maturity patterns — and what they predict
P1
The inverted pyramid — strong front, hollow back
Civic operating categories (1–6) score significantly higher than institutional support categories (7–13). The institution delivers effectively for its community but lacks the organizational infrastructure to sustain that delivery. This is the most common pattern in young, mission-driven institutions founded by community-connected individuals with strong service skills and limited organizational development experience.
What it predicts: Burnout of founding staff, capability collapse on key person departures, inconsistent service delivery as the organization scales, governance crises when leadership succession is required. The civic operating scores are typically sustained by individual heroism rather than institutional process — and heroism is not a sustainable organizational resource.
P2
The bureaucratic shell — strong back, hollow front
Institutional support categories score higher than civic operating categories. The institution has well-developed organizational infrastructure but delivers limited community value. This pattern is most common in organizations that began as advocacy or policy bodies and expanded into service delivery, or in organizations that have absorbed significant organizational development investment from funders without equivalent investment in community-facing capability.
What it predicts: Funder-community accountability gap, declining community engagement and trust, mission drift toward what the institution is organizationally comfortable doing rather than what the community needs. The organizational infrastructure is an asset — but it is currently pointed inward rather than outward.
P3
The fragile peak — one or two exceptional categories amid widespread weakness
One or two categories score at Level 3 or 4 while the majority of categories score at Level 1. The institution has a genuine area of excellence — often its founding capability, the specific program or service that generated its initial impact and reputation — surrounded by underdeveloped infrastructure. The peak score is real and valuable; the surrounding weakness is both invisible and destabilizing.
What it predicts: Overextension of the exceptional capability beyond the infrastructure that can support it; institutional identity so tightly bound to the peak capability that alternatives cannot be explored; vulnerability to external shocks that disrupt the specific context in which the peak capability operates. The fragile peak institution is often described as "brilliant but brittle."
P4
The novel gap — strong adapted categories, absent novel categories
Categories 1–13 score reasonably across the board, but Categories 14, 15, and 16 score at Level 1. The institution has built solid operational capability using adapted enterprise process frameworks but has not yet confronted the genuinely novel process demands of post-labor institutional design. This pattern is increasingly common as institutions mature past their founding phase and begin to scale.
What it predicts: AI systems deployed without governance or member accountability (Category 14); institutional success measured by service delivery metrics that cannot answer whether members' lives are improving (Category 15); democratic participation that is formal rather than genuine, gradually hollowing out institutional legitimacy (Category 16). The novel gap institutions are operationally sophisticated and philosophically incomplete.
P5
The plateau — consistent Level 2 across most categories
Most categories score at Level 2: processes are defined, owned, and producing consistent outputs most of the time, but have not achieved the measured, managed consistency of Level 3. This is the institutional equivalent of being good enough — the organization delivers, survives, and maintains community trust, but nothing improves systematically and the organization cannot teach what it does to others. The plateau is stable but not dynamic.
What it predicts: Gradual institutional aging — the processes become increasingly tailored to the current context and less adaptable to new conditions; slow erosion of community relevance as needs evolve and institutional responses do not; loss of organizational energy as staff and members experience competent but uninspiring organizational life. The move from Level 2 to Level 3 — from defined to consistently managed — requires measurement infrastructure that most plateau institutions have never built.
The maturity model as an ongoing compass

The most common mistake institutions make with maturity assessments is treating them as one-time events: something done at founding or when a funder asks for it, then filed and forgotten. This is a significant waste of the assessment's value. The maturity model is most useful as a recurring tool — run annually, or after significant organizational changes, and used to track developmental progress over time.

Progress on the maturity model is inherently slow. Moving a category from Level 1 to Level 2 typically requires six to twelve months of deliberate development work. Moving from Level 2 to Level 3 typically requires one to two years. These are not timelines to be demoralized by — they are realistic expectations for what institutional development actually takes, and they explain why the maturity model is a compass rather than a destination. The goal is not to reach Level 4 across all 16 categories. The goal is to build the categories that matter most to your institution's mission and vulnerability profile to the level at which they stop generating active risk and begin contributing genuine institutional strength.

An institution that runs the assessment annually and tracks its profile over three years will see something that no single assessment can show: a trajectory. A trajectory toward Level 2 and 3 in the categories most critical to its mission, with deliberate decisions about which categories to develop and which to accept at lower maturity until capacity permits further development. That trajectory — the evidence that the institution is getting better at what matters most — is the most powerful signal that the work of institutional design is being done well.

The goal is not to reach Level 4 across 16 categories. It is to build the categories that matter most to your mission to the level where they stop generating active risk — and then to move the horizon forward, one deliberate development cycle at a time.

The interactive assessment tool: The maturity assessment is available in interactive form at ple-gps.netlify.app. The tool allows individual and group scoring, generates a visual profile, provides context-specific development guidance for each maturity gap, and allows institutions to track their profile over time. The printed rubric in Appendix C is a complete offline version for workshop use without digital access.

On honesty in self-assessment: The most common source of inaccurate maturity assessments is motivated reasoning — the tendency to score categories higher than warranted because lower scores feel like institutional failures. The frame that produces more accurate assessments is the developmental one: a Level 1 score is not a verdict that the institution has failed to do something important; it is useful information about where development investment is needed. Institutions that approach the assessment with curiosity rather than defensiveness consistently produce more accurate profiles and more actionable development plans.

On external facilitation: For institutions conducting their first group assessment, external facilitation by someone familiar with the GPS framework significantly improves the quality of the discussion. Not because external facilitators know the institution better than its members — they do not — but because they can hold the space for productive disagreement without the institutional politics that might cause internal facilitators to smooth over the most diagnostically valuable conflicts.
