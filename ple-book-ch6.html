Chapter 6

The 16 categories — a guided tour of the PLE-GPS

A complete walkthrough of the taxonomy — what each category contains, why it is where it is, and what it looks like in real institutions

A taxonomy is a claim about what things belong together. The 16 categories of the PLE-GPS are not an arbitrary grouping of related activities — they are an argument about how the work of a post-labor institution is organized, what each domain of work is for, and how the domains relate to each other. This chapter is a guided tour of that argument: a complete walkthrough of all 16 categories that explains the reasoning behind each one, describes its key process groups, illustrates it with examples from real institutions, and connects it to the dual pyramid framework introduced in Chapter 3.

When the APQC published the first version of its Process Classification Framework in 1992, the assumption was that the vocabulary problem — organizations inventing incompatible names for the same work — was costing organizations more in translation than in improvement. That turned out to be correct: organizations that adopted shared process language cut their benchmarking costs by roughly half. The same problem exists in the solidarity economy. Each category in the chapters that follow Part II receives its own full treatment — theory, process depth, maturity model, case studies, and implementation guidance. This chapter is not a substitute for that depth. It is a map of the territory before you enter it: oriented, labeled, and legible enough that when you reach a specific category chapter, you already understand where it sits in relation to everything else. It is also, for the reader who needs to make decisions about priorities before reading the full chapters, a sufficient basis for that prioritization.

The 16 categories are organized in three tiers. The civic operating tier (categories 1–6) describes what institutions do for and with their communities: the visible, outward-facing work. The institutional support tier (categories 7–13) describes what institutions do to sustain themselves: the operational infrastructure beneath the surface. The novel PLE tier (categories 14–16) describes what post-labor institutions must do that no institutional framework has previously attempted to operationalize. Each tier has its own color register in this chapter and throughout the book: teal for civic operating, blue for institutional support, violet for the novel categories.

Civic operating processes · categories 1–6 What institutions do for and with communities

The civic operating tier covers the work that communities can see. It is the front half of the institution: the processes through which purpose is translated into collective action, public goods are designed and maintained, people gain access to what the institution provides, resources and services are delivered, and members receive direct support. An institution can have excellent support infrastructure and still fail if these six categories are underdeveloped — because this is where the institution meets the world it exists to serve.

1.0
Collective vision & governance strategy
Defining purpose, establishing governance, and holding the institution accountable to what it said it would do
Foundation Prosperity L3 Power L3
Category 1.0 is the institution's north star — and its mirror. It contains the processes through which an institution decides what it is for, how decisions will be made and by whom, how performance against purpose will be measured, and how power within the institution will be organized, distributed, and held accountable. It sits at the peak of both pyramids simultaneously: it is where the Pyramid of Power's distributed governance aspiration is translated into specific governance architecture, and where the Pyramid of Prosperity's individual agency level is connected back to collective purpose. Without a developed Category 1.0, everything the institution builds is oriented toward an implicit rather than explicit goal — which means it cannot be evaluated, contested, or improved.

1.2

1.3

1.5

In practice: The Mondragon Corporation's annual General Assembly, where worker-owners set strategic direction, approve financial plans, and elect governance bodies — this is Category 1.0 at scale. A small mutual aid network's monthly all-hands call, where the group decides whether to take on a new neighborhood, is Category 1.0 at its simplest. The processes are different; the function is identical.

2.0
Design & steward public goods
Designing, deploying, and maintaining the commons — infrastructure, knowledge, and services that benefit everyone
Core Output Prosperity L2
If Category 1.0 answers "what are we for," Category 2.0 answers "what do we actually make." It contains the processes through which an institution designs, builds, maintains, and improves the public goods and commons infrastructure at the center of its mission. This is not product management translated into community language — it is a fundamentally different design orientation. Where product management optimizes for customer value and revenue capture, public goods stewardship optimizes for collective benefit, durability, and equitable access. The processes are structurally analogous but teleologically opposed. Category 2.0 is where Elinor Ostrom's principles for sustainable commons governance — clear boundaries, collective choice arrangements, graduated sanctions, nested governance — become operational rather than theoretical.

Wikipedia's content governance processes, including verifiability standards, editorial dispute resolution, and access policy, represent Category 2.0 applied to a knowledge commons at global scale.

3.0
Facilitate access & engagement
Ensuring that the people who most need what the institution provides can actually reach it
Reach Power L1
An institution that exists but cannot be reached by the people it is meant to serve is not an institution — it is a document. Category 3.0 contains the processes through which institutions translate good intentions into genuine access: removing barriers, designing onboarding that works for the specific communities served, communicating in ways that reach rather than broadcast, and maintaining ongoing engagement that keeps members connected to governance and services over time. This category is systematically underdeveloped in most solidarity economy institutions, which invest heavily in designing what they offer and lightly in ensuring that those with the greatest need can navigate to it. The category maps to the foundation of the Power pyramid: transparency and access as the precondition for participation.

In practice: The Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative in Boston maintains extensive multilingual outreach, transportation support, and childcare provisions for all governance events — recognizing that participation without these supports is participation for those who already have resources. This is Category 3.0 operationalized as a budget line, not an aspiration.

4.0
Deliver physical resources
Planning, procuring, and distributing the material things that make lives livable
Operations Prosperity L1
Food, housing, tools, medical supplies, transportation, technology equipment — the physical layer of post-labor community support. Category 4.0 contains the processes through which institutions plan, procure, store, distribute, and maintain physical resources without the price system as an allocation mechanism. This is not supply chain management for an enterprise: there are no customers with purchasing power sorting themselves by willingness to pay, no market signal indicating where scarcity is most acute. Allocation decisions that the market makes automatically — and badly, from an equity standpoint — must be made deliberately, through community-determined criteria and processes that can be explained and contested. This requires specific process design that most institutions operating in this space have developed only partially and informally.
4.1

4.2
Manage inventory, storage, and distribution logistics
4.3
Deliver housing and shelter resources and stewardship
4.4
Deliver food, nutrition, and related resources
4.5
Maintain, repair, and renew physical assets for community use
In practice: Cooperation Jackson in Mississippi manages a network of urban farms, community land, and fabrication facilities — procuring inputs, maintaining equipment, and distributing outputs to members and the surrounding community. Their allocation protocols, developed through community deliberation, specify who receives what and how scarcity is managed when demand exceeds supply. This is Category 4.0 built with genuine operational rigor.

5.0
Deliver services & programs
Designing and iterating on the service offerings that address community needs over time
Operations Prosperity L1
Where Category 4.0 delivers things, Category 5.0 delivers processes: education, skills development, health services, economic empowerment programs, legal aid, digital literacy, mediation, and the range of other service offerings through which institutions address community needs that cannot be met with physical goods alone. The design challenge in Category 5.0 is the feedback loop: post-labor institutions must design services that are responsive to expressed community need, must iterate on those designs as needs evolve and as service delivery reveals unforeseen gaps, and must do this in an environment without the market's blunt but responsive feedback mechanism of purchase and abandonment. The service design and iteration processes that replace the market signal are among the most important — and most often absent — processes in this category.

5.2
Deliver educational and workforce development programs
5.3
Deliver health, wellness, and prevention services
5.4

5.5

In practice: The Highlander Research and Education Center designs and iterates on leadership development programs that have shaped social movements for nearly a century — with a feedback methodology that explicitly asks participants what changed in their communities, not what they learned in the program. Process 5.5 (evaluate and iterate) is the process that makes programs like Highlander's become more effective over time rather than calcifying around founding assumptions.

6.0
Support community members
When the institution meets the individual — case management, crisis response, and the dignity of direct support
Service Prosperity L1
The most personal category in the civic operating tier: the processes through which institutions engage with individual members facing specific difficulties, crises, or needs that require more than general services can provide. Case management, crisis intervention, peer support facilitation, grievance handling, and the tracking of individual outcomes over time — these are processes that require both operational rigor and profound attention to human dignity. The tension between systematizing support (which makes it consistent, accountable, and scalable) and individualizing it (which makes it responsive and dignified) is the central design challenge of Category 6.0. Institutions that resolve this tension poorly either produce dehumanizing bureaucratic case processing or unsustainable informal support that burns out the humans providing it.
6.1
Provide direct member support and case management
6.2
Manage grievances, appeals, and conflict resolution
6.3
Deliver wraparound and crisis support services
6.4
Facilitate peer support networks and mutual aid within the community
6.5
Track and report on individual member outcomes and wellbeing
In practice: The Denver Basic Income Project discovered that their most important Category 6.0 processes were not their formal case management systems but the peer navigators — people with lived experience of homelessness who served as relationship-based guides for participants. The formal processes (6.1, 6.3) provided consistency; the peer support process (6.4) provided the trust without which the formal processes could not function. Both are required; neither is sufficient alone.

Institutional support processes · categories 7–13 How institutions sustain themselves

The institutional support tier is what the civic operating tier stands on. These seven categories — human development, technology, finance, commons management, integrity, external relations, and continuous improvement — are the operational infrastructure that allows the visible work to happen consistently and improve over time. They are systematically underfunded and underdeveloped in post-labor institutions, because they are invisible when they function and catastrophic when they fail. A mutual aid network without Category 9.0 (collective resources) collapses when a grant ends. A cooperative without Category 11.0 (integrity) slides into insider governance without the founders noticing. A commons institution without Category 13.0 (capabilities) runs its founding program on autopilot while the community it serves moves on.

7.0
Develop human potential
People are the institution — how to recruit, develop, and sustain them without competitive labor markets to do the work
People Prosperity L3
Post-labor institutions cannot compete on salary for talent in most domains. They compete on meaning, mission, community, and the quality of the work environment they create. Category 7.0 contains the processes through which institutions build these alternative forms of talent value: values-aligned recruitment, genuine skill development and advancement paths, peer recognition systems, cultures that sustain rather than consume the people who inhabit them, and succession planning that treats institutional knowledge as a commons to be preserved rather than a proprietary asset held by individuals. The burnout rate in solidarity economy organizations is a Category 7.0 failure above all else — it is the predictable result of institutions that recruit on mission and invest nothing in sustaining the humans who accepted the offer.
7.1
Recruit and onboard mission-aligned staff and contributors
7.2
Design and deliver skills development and advancement pathways
7.3
Manage performance, recognition, and contribution acknowledgment
7.4
Foster institutional culture, values, and psychological safety
7.5
Manage succession, transitions, and knowledge transfer for departures
In practice: Organic Valley cooperative built a comprehensive peer mentorship and career development program specifically because they recognized that staff who could earn 30% more elsewhere were staying for something that compensation could not reproduce — and that protecting that something required active investment. Their Category 7.4 and 7.5 processes are as developed as their operational service delivery, which is unusual and intentional.
8.0
Technology & AI infrastructure
Digital systems, data stewardship, and AI tooling as commons infrastructure — serving members, not extracting from them
Technology Power L1
Technology in post-labor institutions is not a support function — it is a governance question. Who controls the data? Whose interests does the technology serve? What happens when an AI system makes a decision that affects a member's access to services? These questions are not technical; they are political. Category 8.0 contains the processes through which institutions make technology serve members rather than extracting value from them: digital access equity, data governance for member benefit, cybersecurity as member protection, and — critically — AI tooling governance as a sub-process domain that has no enterprise precedent but is essential to any institution that uses algorithmic systems in its operations. The full AI governance framework lives in Category 14.0; Category 8.0 handles the operational technology management that enables and constrains how AI is deployed.
8.1
Manage digital systems, platforms, and operational technology
8.2
Govern data architecture, quality, and member data stewardship
8.3
Manage cybersecurity and member privacy protection
8.4
Deploy, manage, and evaluate AI and automation tooling
8.5
Ensure equitable digital access for all members and participants
In practice: The Internet Archive's digital preservation infrastructure is Category 8.0 operating at institutional scale — managing petabytes of data as a knowledge commons with explicit data stewardship principles, access equity mandates, and governance processes that treat digital infrastructure as belonging to the public rather than to the organization that happens to be maintaining it.
9.0
Steward collective resources
Financial management without the profit motive — when sustainability means sustaining the mission, not the balance sheet
Finance Prosperity L2
Money is as politically charged inside solidarity economy institutions as anywhere else — perhaps more so, because the institutions cannot appeal to the neutral authority of shareholder value to depoliticize financial decisions. Category 9.0 contains the processes through which institutions manage this charged territory: participatory budgeting, mission-aligned treasury management, diversified community revenue development, and resource allocation through democratic rather than technocratic processes. The category is structurally similar to enterprise finance but fundamentally different in its objective function: not to maximize return on investment but to maximize mission impact within the constraint of institutional sustainability. The processes for doing this — particularly for participatory allocation and community wealth building — have no enterprise equivalent and require specific design.
9.1
Develop and manage institutional budgets and financial strategy
9.2
Manage accounting, financial reporting, and audit
9.3
Steward treasury, reserves, and endowment for long-term sustainability
9.4
Develop and diversify community revenue and resource flows
9.5
Allocate resources through participatory and mission-aligned processes
In practice: Participatory budgeting in Porto Alegre, Brazil — where residents directly decide the allocation of a significant portion of the municipal budget — is Category 9.5 at governmental scale. At the institutional level, the Catalan Integral Cooperative's internal economy, which uses a mutual credit system to reduce dependence on the euro while keeping resource allocation transparent and member-governed, represents Category 9.0 built with genuine philosophical coherence.
10.0
Manage commons & shared assets
Governing what belongs to everyone — land, equipment, intellectual property, and the productive assets of the community
Commons Prosperity L2
This category is the operational home of commons governance: the processes through which collectively owned assets — land, buildings, equipment, vehicles, tools, intellectual property, digital infrastructure — are managed in ways that sustain their availability for collective use over time. It is here that Ostrom's principles become operational procedures: defining who can access what and under what conditions, maintaining assets so they remain usable, resolving conflicts when access claims compete, and governing the addition of new assets to the commons. Category 10.0 is closely related to Category 2.0 (design and steward public goods), but where 2.0 focuses on the design and stewardship of goods and services, 10.0 focuses on the governance and maintenance of the physical and digital assets that those goods and services depend on.
10.1
Govern collectively owned land, property, and real estate
10.2
Manage shared equipment, tools, and productive assets
10.3
Steward intellectual commons, open source, and shared IP
10.4
Design and administer commons access protocols and usage rules
10.5
Maintain, insure, and renew shared physical and digital assets
In practice: The Champlain Housing Trust in Burlington, Vermont — the largest community land trust in the United States — manages over 2,500 homes with governance processes for resale pricing, tenant selection, maintenance standards, and long-term stewardship that have been refined over four decades. Their Category 10.0 processes are among the most thoroughly documented in the solidarity economy.
11.0
Institutional integrity & equity
Accountability without hierarchy — how institutions stay honest when there is no external market to discipline them
Governance Power L1 Both pyramids
Every institution — regardless of its values, its governance structure, or its founding intentions — is subject to the concentration of power, the drift from stated mission, the gradual replacement of community accountability with insider accountability. Category 11.0 is the immune system: the processes through which institutions maintain the conditions necessary for their own integrity over time. Compliance and legal management, ethics oversight, equity auditing, public transparency, and the investigation and remediation of integrity violations — these are not external impositions on mission-driven institutions but the operational backbone without which any stated mission eventually hollows out. The history of the nonprofit sector and the cooperative movement is littered with institutions that articulated genuine values and failed to build the processes that would have held them to those values under pressure.
11.1
Manage legal compliance, regulatory requirements, and fiduciary duties
11.2
Conduct ethics oversight, anti-corruption controls, and code enforcement
11.3
Perform equity auditing, bias review, and inclusion assessment
11.4
Manage public transparency, disclosure, and community reporting
11.5
Investigate and remediate integrity violations and grievances
In practice: The Cleveland Clinic's Office of Patient Experience is not a perfect analog, but its structure — independent investigation, mandatory reporting, and public disclosure of adverse outcomes — illustrates what Category 11.5 can look like when taken seriously. In solidarity economy contexts, worker cooperative federations like USFWC have developed ethics and conflict resolution protocols that serve a similar function across member institutions.
12.0
Inter-institutional relations
No post-labor institution survives in isolation — the processes of federation, coalition, and mutual support
Network Power L3
The solidarity economy is not a collection of individual institutions — it is a network, and its strength depends entirely on the quality of the connections between its nodes. Category 12.0 contains the processes through which individual institutions participate in that network: building partnerships, joining and contributing to federations and coalitions, managing relationships with government and enabling organizations, coordinating mutual support with peer institutions, and representing institutional interests in the broader movement. This category is the operational implementation of the third level of the Power pyramid — distributed governance — at the inter-institutional scale. Institutions that invest heavily in Categories 1–11 but neglect Category 12.0 build organizational strength that cannot compound through solidarity, and remain vulnerable to political and economic pressures that networked institutions can collectively resist.
12.1
Build and steward strategic partnerships and coalition relationships
12.2
Participate in and contribute to federated networks and alliances
12.3
Manage government, regulatory, and enabling organization relationships
12.4
Coordinate peer support, resource sharing, and mutual aid with allied institutions
12.5
Represent institutional interests in movement politics and broader advocacy
In practice: The Emilia-Romagna cooperative network in northern Italy — where over 15,000 cooperatives participate in federated structures that share capital, provide technical assistance, and coordinate political advocacy — is the most developed example of Category 12.0 in operation. The network's resilience through economic crises that have devastated comparable private-sector economies is directly attributable to the inter-institutional processes that individual cooperatives could not have built alone.
13.0
Evolve institutional capabilities
The institution that cannot learn cannot last — continuous improvement, R&D, and knowledge stewardship
Growth Both pyramids
Every institution is subject to the same force: the world changes faster than organizational practices do. Category 13.0 is the mechanism by which institutions respond — not reactively, in crisis, but continuously, through deliberate processes of learning, improvement, and adaptation. Continuous improvement methodology, internal research and development, knowledge management and documentation, organizational learning culture, and the processes for managing deliberate transformation — these are the processes that determine whether an institution improves over time or calcifies. Without Category 13.0, even institutions with excellent current processes slowly become less effective as conditions change and as the gap between institutional practice and community need quietly widens.
13.1
Drive continuous improvement and process optimization
13.2
Manage institutional research, development, and experimentation
13.3
Capture, organize, and share institutional knowledge
13.4
Facilitate organizational learning and adaptive culture
13.5
Plan and manage deliberate institutional transformation
In practice: The Mondragon cooperatives' internal university (Mondragón Unibertsitatea) is Category 13.0 institutionalized at the network level — a mechanism for continuous workforce development, applied research, and organizational learning that treats improvement as a permanent institutional function rather than a response to crisis. At the individual institution level, the practice of retrospective review after every major program cycle — common in tech organizations and rare in the solidarity economy — is the simplest possible implementation of Category 13.4.
Novel PLE categories · categories 14–16 What only post-labor institutions need

The three novel categories aren't refinements of existing institutional thinking. They are the categories that mark post-labor institutions as genuinely different from every institutional form that preceded them. No enterprise framework, no nonprofit management standard, no cooperative development guide has attempted to operationalize these three domains as institutional processes. Their absence from the existing literature is not an oversight — it is a reflection of the fact that enterprises do not need them. Post-labor institutions do. And the distance between "having values about" these three domains and "having processes for" them is precisely the design gap this book was written to close.

14.0
AI governance & algorithmic accountability
When the algorithm governs you — oversight, auditing, and member rights in an AI-mediated institution
Novel Power L2 No enterprise analog
Post-labor institutions will use AI systems extensively — for resource allocation, service eligibility determination, case management support, communication, governance facilitation, and an expanding range of functions. Each of these AI applications will make decisions, or support decisions, that affect members' lives. Category 14.0 is the process infrastructure for ensuring that those decisions are accountable to the members affected by them: ethics policy and oversight structures, bias and harm auditing, informed consent processes, ongoing monitoring for model drift, and governance mechanisms for approving or rejecting AI applications within the institution. This category sits at the second level of the Power pyramid because algorithmic accountability is fundamentally a democratic question — not a technical one. Who decides what the algorithm optimizes for? Who has the right to contest an algorithmic decision? Who is accountable when the system produces harmful outcomes? These are governance questions that require governance processes.
14.1
Establish AI ethics policies, principles, and institutional oversight structures
14.2
Audit AI and algorithmic systems for bias, harm, and fairness
14.3
Manage informed consent for AI-influenced decisions affecting members
14.4
Monitor AI system performance, drift, and unintended consequences
14.5
Govern AI procurement, deployment, and member rights to explanation and appeal
In practice: No solidarity economy institution has yet developed Category 14.0 processes at full maturity — this is genuinely frontier work. The closest precedents are the AI auditing frameworks developed by civil society organizations (the Algorithmic Justice League, the AI Now Institute) and the emerging regulatory frameworks in the EU AI Act. The GPS translates these into institutional-level operational processes rather than external regulatory requirements.

15.0
Human flourishing & wellbeing
The terminal question — measuring and cultivating what post-labor institutions are ultimately for
Novel Prosperity L3 No enterprise analog
This is the most philosophically demanding category in the framework — and the most consequential. Category 15.0 asks post-labor institutions to do something no enterprise has ever been required to do: define what human flourishing means for their specific community, develop methods for measuring it, design programs that actively cultivate it, and hold themselves accountable to it as the terminal metric against which all other institutional activity is evaluated. This is not a wellness program or an employee assistance initiative. It is the institutional operationalization of the insight at the heart of post-labor economics: that the purpose of economic activity is human flourishing, that employment was always only a means to that end, and that in a world where employment as a means is becoming unreliable, the end must be addressed directly. The hardest process in this category is 15.1 — defining flourishing — because it requires the kind of community deliberation that produces genuine philosophical disagreement and cannot be resolved by measurement alone.
15.1
Define and deliberate community-specific flourishing indicators and frameworks
15.2
Design programs explicitly oriented toward meaning, purpose, and self-determination
15.3
Build and maintain mental, emotional, and social health infrastructure
15.4
Cultivate conditions for social connection, belonging, and community
15.5
Measure, report on, and improve collective wellbeing outcomes
In practice: Bhutan's Gross National Happiness framework — which measures flourishing across nine domains including psychological wellbeing, cultural vitality, time use, and governance quality — is Category 15.0 at a national scale. At the institutional level, the Basic Income Earth Network's longitudinal research tracking wellbeing outcomes across multiple dimensions (not just income) is the closest precedent for what a well-developed 15.5 process looks like.

16.0
Participatory democracy
Voice as infrastructure, not aspiration — designing and operating the systems through which members govern their institution
Novel Power L2 No enterprise analog
Participatory democracy is one of the most frequently stated values and least operationalized practices in the solidarity economy. Category 16.0 is the demand that this change: that democratic participation be treated as an operational process with designed systems, trained facilitators, documented procedures, performance measurement, and continuous improvement — not as a cultural aspiration that is realized whenever someone holds a vote or opens a comment period. The processes in this category — voting and consent mechanism design, deliberation infrastructure, participatory budgeting, citizen assembly management, and democratic mandate renewal — are the architectural elements of a self-governing institution. Together, they are the second level of the Power pyramid made operational. Without them, the distributed governance aspiration of the pyramid's third level remains permanently aspirational.
16.1
Design, implement, and manage member voting and consent mechanisms
16.2
Facilitate structured deliberation and consensus-building processes
16.3
Manage participatory budgeting and member resource decision processes
16.4
Convene and facilitate citizen assemblies and community deliberative bodies
16.5
Manage democratic mandate renewal, governance review, and legitimacy maintenance
In practice: The Irish Citizens' Assemblies — which produced the deliberative groundwork for Ireland's marriage equality and abortion referenda — are Category 16.4 at national scale. At the institutional level, the Loomio cooperative's governance software, developed specifically to enable asynchronous deliberation and consent-based decision-making in organizations of any size, represents the kind of process infrastructure that Category 16.1 and 16.2 require to function at scale without face-to-face meeting as the only mechanism.

How the 16 categories flow together

The categories aren't isolated domains! They form a system: the civic operating tier depends on the institutional support tier for the infrastructure that makes its work sustainable; the novel categories permeate both tiers as oversight, measurement, and participation mechanisms that run through all 13 adapted categories. Understanding the most important inter-category flows is essential for diagnosing institutional underperformance — most institutional failures are produced not by weakness in a single category but by a break in a critical inter-category dependency.

The 16 categories are not a checklist. They are an architecture — a claim about how the work of building a post-labor institution is organized, what each domain serves, and how the failure of any one creates pressure that eventually ruptures somewhere else in the system.

The tour is complete. The chapters ahead go deep — one category at a time, with the theory, the process detail, the case studies, and the implementation guidance that a quick walkthrough can't provide. But every deep dive in Parts III, IV, and V will make more sense for having first seen the whole. The map is drawn. The territory begins on the next page.

On the examples cited: All real institutions mentioned in this chapter are cited as illustrations of specific process domains, not as endorsements of the institution's overall approach or performance. Every institution has underdeveloped areas in the GPS framework, and the examples are selected to illustrate specific process strengths rather than to characterize any institution's overall quality.

On the KPIs listed: The KPI examples in each category card are illustrative — drawn from the full 229-KPI library in Appendix B. A complete KPI set for any category includes both leading and lagging indicators, quantitative and qualitative measures, and member-facing and institutional metrics. The full library is the authoritative reference; the examples here are orientation only.

On category boundaries: The boundaries between adjacent categories involve genuine judgment calls — the line between Category 2.0 (steward public goods) and Category 10.0 (manage commons assets) is real but porous; the line between Category 6.0 (support members) and Category 15.0 (human flourishing) is permeable by design. Where processes could legitimately belong to either of two adjacent categories, the placement reflects the dominant function — strategic stewardship in 2.0, operational asset governance in 10.0; direct individual support in 6.0, systemic flourishing measurement in 15.0. In practice, institutions will develop these adjacent categories in conversation with each other, and the exact boundary matters less than the question of whether both functions are being developed.
