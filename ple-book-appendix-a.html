Tier I — Civic operating processes (GPS 1.0–6.0)
GPS 1.0Collective Vision & Governance Strategy
5 process groups22 processesCivic Operating
Scope
Covers: the processes through which a post-labor institution defines its collective mission, designs governance architecture, establishes decision rights, manages stakeholder relationships, and monitors governance health over time. Does not cover: operational delivery of programs (GPS 5.0), financial governance (GPS 9.0), or democratic participation processes beyond governance design (GPS 16.0).
1.1
Develop collective vision and institutional mission
Processes through which the institution articulates and maintains its foundational purpose — developed with community participation, reviewed periodically, and translated into operational direction.
1.1.1
Facilitate participatory mission development — community members, staff, and governance body co-create mission through structured process
1.1.1.1
Design and convene community visioning sessions with structured facilitation
1.1.1.2
Synthesize community input into draft mission language
1.1.1.3
Ratify mission through governance body approval with community endorsement process
1.1.2
Develop theory of change — explicit causal model linking institutional activities to intended community outcomes
1.1.2.1
Map community conditions the institution addresses and mechanisms through which it addresses them
1.1.2.2
Identify assumptions underlying the theory of change and surface them explicitly
1.1.2.3
Review and update theory of change in response to evaluation findings (minimum every 3 years)
1.1.3
Maintain mission alignment over time — periodic reassessment of whether institutional activities remain aligned with stated mission
1.1.3.1
Conduct annual mission alignment review at governance body level
1.1.3.2
Assess whether resource allocation reflects mission priorities
1.1.3.3
Manage mission drift — identify and address programmatic expansion beyond mission scope
1.1.4
Communicate institutional identity — articulate mission, values, and theory of change to all stakeholders in accessible language
1.1.4.1
Develop plain-language mission communications for community members without organizational background
1.1.4.2
Create onboarding materials that convey institutional identity to new staff, governance members, and partners
1.2
Design and maintain governance architecture
Processes for establishing the structures, bodies, and roles through which collective governance is exercised — from founding governance design through ongoing governance body management and succession.
1.2.1
Design governance body structure — size, composition, term limits, election or appointment mechanisms, and role of community members
1.2.1.1
Determine governance body size and composition requirements including community member representation minimums
1.2.1.2
Design election or appointment processes that produce demographically representative governance bodies
1.2.1.3
Establish term limits and staggered succession to prevent governance concentration
1.2.2
Govern governance body operations — meeting structures, quorum requirements, decision protocols, and documentation
1.2.2.1
Design governance meeting agendas for substantive deliberation rather than information receipt
1.2.2.2
Maintain accurate records of governance decisions and their rationale
1.2.2.3
Manage governance body committees and their delegated authorities
1.2.3
Develop governance body members — orientation, ongoing education, and leadership development for governance roles
1.2.3.1
Design and deliver governance member onboarding covering institutional history, financials, strategy, and governance obligations
1.2.3.2
Provide ongoing governance education on topics relevant to current institutional challenges
1.2.3.3
Conduct annual governance self-assessment and identify development priorities
1.2.4
Manage governance succession — planned transitions in governance body composition and leadership roles
1.2.4.1
Maintain a governance succession plan identifying roles at risk and candidates in development
1.2.4.2
Design executive director succession process including governance body authority and community input
1.3
Govern policy, decision rights, and institutional bylaws
Processes for establishing, maintaining, and enforcing the policy framework within which institutional decisions are made — including bylaw development, decision authority mapping, and policy compliance.
1.3.1
Develop and maintain institutional bylaws — foundational legal governance documents
1.3.1.1
Draft bylaws with legal counsel and community input covering all required governance provisions
1.3.1.2
Review and update bylaws at minimum every five years or following major governance changes
1.3.2
Map and maintain decision rights — explicit specification of which decisions are made by which governance bodies
1.3.2.1
Develop a decision authority matrix covering all major institutional decision categories
1.3.2.2
Communicate decision rights to all staff, governance members, and community participants
1.3.2.3
Review decision rights annually and adjust as institutional complexity evolves
1.3.3
Develop and maintain institutional policy framework — policies governing employment, financial management, communications, data, and ethics
1.3.3.1
Maintain complete, current policy inventory with review dates and ownership assignments
1.3.3.2
Conduct annual policy compliance review across all major policy areas
1.4
Manage stakeholder relationships and constituent voice
Processes for identifying, engaging, and maintaining relationships with all stakeholder groups whose interests the institution affects — distinguishing governance stakeholders, funding stakeholders, and community constituents.
1.4.1
Map and analyze stakeholders — identify all parties with significant stake in institutional decisions
1.4.1.1
Conduct stakeholder mapping identifying all parties affected by or able to affect institutional decisions
1.4.1.2
Assess stakeholder interests, influence levels, and alignment with institutional mission
1.4.2
Design differential stakeholder engagement — engagement strategies proportionate to stakeholder impact and influence
1.4.2.1
Develop engagement plan for each major stakeholder group specifying communication frequency, format, and decision involvement level
1.4.2.2
Manage funder relationships with explicit boundaries on funder influence over institutional direction
1.4.3
Ensure constituent voice in governance — mechanisms through which the community served has proportionate influence over institutional direction
1.4.3.1
Design formal constituent voice mechanisms as distinct from informal consultation
1.4.3.2
Track and report how constituent input has influenced institutional decisions
1.5
Monitor governance health and conduct governance assessment
Processes for systematically assessing whether governance structures are performing as designed — producing genuine oversight, authentic participation, and accountable institutional behavior.
1.5.1
Conduct governance body self-assessment — annual structured reflection on effectiveness, culture, and development needs
1.5.1.1
Design and administer governance self-assessment survey covering effectiveness, culture, and individual member contribution
1.5.1.2
Discuss assessment findings in governance session and develop improvement priorities
1.5.2
Assess executive director performance — annual governance body assessment against institutional commitments
1.5.2.1
Conduct structured annual executive director assessment using commitment metrics with 360-degree input
1.5.2.2
Connect assessment findings to compensation decisions and development support
1.5.3
Govern governance reform — processes for changing governance structures when assessment reveals inadequacy
1.5.3.1
Design governance reform processes with community input for major structural changes
1.5.3.2
Implement governance changes with appropriate transition planning and stakeholder communication
Key interfaces
GPS 11.0 — accountability architecture operationalizes governance commitmentsGPS 13.0 — strategic renewal examines governance model fitnessGPS 16.0 — participatory democracy extends governance authority to community membersGPS 3.0 — access processes ensure governance participation is equitable
GPS 2.0Design & Steward Public Goods
5 process groups20 processesCivic Operating
Scope
Covers: processes through which institutions identify, design, maintain, and steward shared resources and public goods held on behalf of the community — applying Ostrom's commons principles to institutional practice. Does not cover: delivery of individualized services (GPS 5.0), governance of specific physical assets (GPS 10.0), or knowledge management for internal operations (GPS 13.1).
2.1
Identify, define, and scope public goods
Processes for determining which resources the institution holds as public goods, defining their boundaries, and establishing governance frameworks for their stewardship.
2.1.1
Define the public goods the institution holds or stewards — explicit inventory with boundaries and governance scope
2.1.1.1
Conduct public goods inventory mapping all shared resources the institution stewards
2.1.1.2
Define boundaries of each public good — who is included in the commons community and stewardship obligations
2.1.2
Develop commons governance rules — specific rules governing use, contribution, and dispute resolution for each public good
2.1.2.1
Develop use rules proportionate to each commons' characteristics applying Ostrom's design principles
2.1.2.2
Establish graduated sanctions for commons rule violations
2.1.2.3
Design conflict resolution processes accessible to all commons participants
2.1.3
Assess commons health — systematic monitoring of whether the commons is sustainably used and equitably governed
2.1.3.1
Track commons use patterns and assess sustainability of current use levels
2.1.3.2
Monitor equity of commons access across participant demographic subgroups
2.2
Design access and eligibility for public goods
Processes for determining who has access to public goods and on what terms — prioritizing equity, openness, and the removal of barriers that produce inequitable access.
2.2.1
Design eligibility criteria that are mission-aligned and equity-preserving — criteria that expand rather than restrict access for the most marginalized
2.2.1.1
Review eligibility criteria annually for equity impact — assessing who is excluded and whether exclusions are mission-justified
2.2.1.2
Design progressive eligibility that prioritizes greatest need within mission scope
2.2.2
Govern pricing and cost-sharing for public goods — structures that preserve access while sustaining the commons financially
2.2.2.1
Design sliding-scale or income-proportionate pricing where cost-sharing is required
2.2.2.2
Assess annually whether cost structures are preventing access for highest-need community members
2.3
Steward and maintain the commons over time
Processes for the active maintenance, renewal, and long-term stewardship of public goods — ensuring commons resources are sustained for current and future participants.
2.3.1
Develop and implement commons maintenance plans — scheduled and preventive maintenance of physical, digital, and social commons infrastructure
2.3.1.1
Assess commons condition annually and develop multi-year maintenance investment plans
2.3.1.2
Establish reserve funds for commons renewal and major maintenance
2.3.2
Govern commons use to prevent overuse and degradation — monitoring mechanisms that detect approaching commons limits
2.3.2.1
Establish and monitor commons sustainability metrics with governance alert thresholds
2.3.2.2
Design use management interventions that respond to sustainability alerts without inequitably restricting access
2.3.3
Manage commons enclosure risk — actively resisting pressures that would convert public goods to private control
2.3.3.1
Assess annually whether any commons resources are at risk of enclosure through privatization, commodification, or institutional capture
2.3.3.2
Implement legal and governance protections against enclosure for highest-risk commons assets
2.4
Share knowledge and contribute to the knowledge commons
Processes through which the institution treats its own knowledge — evaluation findings, process designs, governance innovations — as commons contributions rather than proprietary assets.
2.4.1
Document and publish institutional learning — making evaluation findings, program models, and governance innovations accessible to peer institutions
2.4.1.1
Publish annual evaluation findings in accessible formats for peer institution use
2.4.1.2
Release program tools, templates, and frameworks under open licenses where appropriate
2.4.2
Actively contribute to field knowledge infrastructure — participating in research, documentation, and knowledge-building that benefits the solidarity economy
2.4.2.1
Participate in sector-level data collection and research initiatives
2.4.2.2
Contribute institutional expertise to peer institution learning through training, facilitation, or mentorship
2.5
Govern public goods transitions — expansion, contraction, and disposition
Processes governing changes to the institution's public goods portfolio — how new public goods are added, how existing ones are wound down, and how transitions protect community interests.
2.5.1
Govern new public goods decisions — process for deciding whether and how to add new commons resources to the institution's stewardship portfolio
2.5.1.1
Design community input process for major new public goods decisions
2.5.1.2
Assess long-term stewardship capacity before accepting new public goods obligations
2.5.2
Govern public goods wind-down — process for responsibly ending stewardship while protecting community members who depend on them
2.5.2.1
Design community notification and transition planning for any public goods disposition
2.5.2.2
Require community approval for any disposition that would convert public goods to private ownership
Key interfaces
GPS 10.0 — commons management at the specific asset levelGPS 4.0 — physical goods distribution for tangible public goodsGPS 11.0 — equity audit of public goods access patternsGPS 12.3 — peer knowledge sharing as commons contribution
GPS 3.0Facilitate Access & Engagement
5 process groups21 processesCivic Operating
Scope
Covers: processes through which community members find, enter, navigate, and engage with institutional resources and governance — including outreach, intake design, barrier removal, engagement infrastructure, and access equity assessment. Does not cover: content of programs and services (GPS 5.0), individualized case support (GPS 6.0), or governance structures through which community members participate in institutional decisions (GPS 16.0).
3.1
Identify and remove access barriers
Systematic identification and elimination of structural, cultural, geographic, linguistic, and economic barriers that prevent community members from accessing institutional resources.
3.1.1
Conduct regular access barrier assessments — systematic identification across six barrier types: physical, linguistic, cultural, economic, informational, and systemic
3.1.1.1
Survey community members about barriers encountered in accessing institutional resources
3.1.1.2
Analyze participation data by demographic subgroup to identify access disparities
3.1.1.3
Conduct physical accessibility audit of all institutional spaces and digital platforms
3.1.2
Design and implement barrier removal interventions — targeted changes that address identified barriers
3.1.2.1
Provide language access services (interpretation, translation) for all service languages in the community
3.1.2.2
Provide childcare, transportation support, or flexible scheduling for programs and governance activities
3.1.2.3
Eliminate means-testing and documentation requirements that create access barriers without equivalent benefit
3.1.3
Design trust-building infrastructure for communities with institutional distrust — practices that address warranted skepticism about institutional intentions
3.1.3.1
Deploy peer connectors and cultural brokers from the community to facilitate initial engagement
3.1.3.2
Design no-commitment, low-stakes first engagement options that allow trust development before program enrollment
3.2
Conduct community outreach and recruitment
Proactive processes for reaching community members who would benefit from institutional resources but are not currently accessing them — prioritizing those with highest need and lowest current access.
3.2.1
Develop targeted outreach strategies for underrepresented populations — identifying and reaching community members who are eligible but not participating
3.2.1.1
Map community demographics against current participation patterns to identify underrepresented groups
3.2.1.2
Partner with trusted community organizations to reach populations with institutional distrust
3.2.2
Design and implement referral networks — relationships with community organizations through which eligible community members are connected to institutional resources
3.2.2.1
Develop warm referral protocols with partner organizations including follow-up tracking
3.2.2.2
Track referral-to-enrollment conversion rates and identify drop-off points
3.3
Manage community communications and information access
Processes for designing and delivering communications that reach community members with accurate, accessible information about institutional resources, decisions, and engagement opportunities.
3.3.1
Design accessible communications — materials and channels that reach community members across language, literacy, digital access, and cultural variation
3.3.1.1
Develop plain-language communications standards and apply them across all community-facing materials
3.3.1.2
Use multiple channels (digital, print, in-person, peer networks) to reach community members across connectivity variation
3.3.2
Manage governance communications — informing community members about institutional decisions and inviting engagement with decision processes
3.3.2.1
Publish governance decisions in accessible language within a defined timeframe after governance body meetings
3.3.2.2
Proactively communicate upcoming decisions that significantly affect community members
3.4
Design engagement pathways and participation infrastructure
Processes for building structured pathways through which community members progress from initial contact through increasing levels of institutional engagement and governance participation.
3.4.1
Map the engagement spectrum and design entry points at multiple levels — ensuring community members can engage at whatever level their current capacity and interest supports
3.4.1.1
Design engagement pathways from low-commitment first contact through active governance participation
3.4.1.2
Create intentional transitions and invitations at each engagement level to encourage progression
3.4.2
Build volunteer and peer engagement infrastructure — systems for recruiting, supporting, and recognizing community members who contribute to institutional operations
3.4.2.1
Develop volunteer roles that provide meaningful contribution opportunities across skill and time availability
3.4.2.2
Design peer navigator and peer connector roles that pay community members for engagement work
3.5
Measure and assess access equity and engagement quality
Processes for systematically evaluating whether community access to institutional resources is equitable across demographic subgroups and whether engagement quality is producing genuine community participation.
3.5.1
Track access and participation metrics disaggregated by demographic subgroup — the primary equity data for GPS 3.0
3.5.1.1
Collect demographic data at intake with community consent and privacy protection
3.5.1.2
Assess participation rates by demographic subgroup against community demographics quarterly
3.5.2
Assess engagement quality — whether participation is producing genuine community agency or nominal involvement
3.5.2.1
Survey community members about their experience of access and engagement quality annually
3.5.2.2
Report access equity findings to governance body with accountability for addressing disparities
Key interfaces
GPS 6.0 — intake processes connect access to individual supportGPS 11.4 — equity audit covers access equity as one of six domainsGPS 16.4 — civic capacity development extends access to governance participation
GPS 4.0Deliver Physical Resources
5 process groups20 processesCivic Operating
Scope
Covers: processes through which institutions source, manage, distribute, and maintain physical resources — food, housing, materials, equipment, and physical infrastructure of community life — with equity and dignity as design requirements throughout. Does not cover: governance of long-term asset ownership (GPS 10.0), financial management of resource acquisition (GPS 9.0), or program contexts in which physical resources are delivered alongside services (GPS 5.0).
4.1
Source and procure physical resources
Processes for identifying, contracting, and building supply relationships for physical resources the institution distributes — with equity, sustainability, and supply chain resilience as explicit procurement requirements.
4.1.1
Develop procurement strategy — mission-aligned sourcing decisions that prioritize local, worker-owned, and community-accountable suppliers
4.1.1.1
Map current supplier relationships and assess alignment with mission and equity values
4.1.1.2
Develop preferred supplier list prioritizing solidarity economy suppliers
4.1.2
Build supply chain resilience — relationships and redundancy ensuring resource continuity during disruption
4.1.2.1
Develop minimum two backup suppliers for each critical resource category
4.1.2.2
Build mutual aid resource-sharing agreements with peer institutions for crisis surge capacity
4.1.3
Assess procurement equity — examining whether procurement decisions create or reproduce supply chain inequities
4.1.3.1
Track supplier demographics and assess whether procurement spend reflects equity commitments
4.2
Manage inventory and supply chain operations
Processes for receiving, storing, tracking, and managing physical resource inventories — including systems for loss prevention, quality control, and alignment between supply and demand.
4.2.1
Design and maintain inventory management systems — tracking systems appropriate to institutional scale that provide visibility into resource availability
4.2.1.1
Implement inventory tracking system scaled to resource volume and distribution complexity
4.2.1.2
Establish reorder points and lead time standards for each critical resource category
4.2.2
Manage storage, handling, and quality assurance — physical infrastructure and practices that maintain resource quality from receipt through distribution
4.2.2.1
Establish quality standards and inspection protocols for received resources
4.2.2.2
Implement storage conditions appropriate to resource type including temperature, security, and access requirements
4.3
Operate distribution and allocation with equity and dignity
Processes for getting physical resources from institutional inventory to community members — designed for dignity of receipt, equitable allocation, and maximum accessibility to those with greatest need.
4.3.1
Design allocation mechanisms that prioritize equity — explicit allocation frameworks rather than first-come, first-served defaults
4.3.1.1
Develop and document the allocation framework specifying priority criteria and tie-breaking rules
4.3.1.2
Communicate allocation criteria to community members in plain language before distribution events
4.3.1.3
Track allocation outcomes by demographic subgroup and assess equity of distribution patterns
4.3.2
Design distribution processes for dignity of receipt — removing practices that stigmatize or surveil recipients
4.3.2.1
Eliminate or minimize means-testing, documentation requirements, and surveillance practices from distribution processes
4.3.2.2
Survey community members about their experience of dignity in distribution processes annually
4.3.3
Manage demand-supply alignment — ensuring resource availability is matched to community need patterns
4.3.3.1
Track demand patterns and forecast resource needs using community data
4.3.3.2
Manage waitlists with equity criteria when demand exceeds supply
4.4
Maintain physical infrastructure supporting resource delivery
Processes for upkeep, repair, and renewal of physical spaces, equipment, and vehicles through which resources are stored, processed, and distributed.
4.4.1
Implement preventive maintenance programs for all physical infrastructure
4.4.1.1
Develop maintenance schedules for all equipment, facilities, and vehicles in the resource delivery system
4.4.1.2
Establish maintenance reserve funding proportional to infrastructure replacement cost
4.4.2
Manage facilities and space for community access — ensuring spaces are accessible, safe, and welcoming across community diversity
4.4.2.1
Conduct annual ADA and accessibility audit of all community-facing spaces
4.4.2.2
Assess community experience of physical spaces for safety, dignity, and cultural inclusiveness
4.5
Govern resource equity and community accountability
Processes ensuring physical resource delivery is accountable to the community — through transparent reporting on allocation patterns, community input on resource priorities, and equity assessment.
4.5.1
Report allocation outcomes transparently — publishing data on who receives what resources under what criteria
4.5.1.1
Publish annual resource allocation report including demographic distribution data
4.5.2
Engage community members in resource priority setting — governance processes through which community shapes what resources the institution prioritizes
4.5.2.1
Conduct annual community resource needs assessment informing procurement and allocation priorities
4.5.2.2
Include community voice in major resource portfolio decisions (adding or eliminating resource categories)

GPS 5.0Deliver Services & Programs
5 process groups22 processesCivic Operating
Scope
Covers: processes through which institutions design, deliver, and evaluate programs and services constituting their primary community benefit work — from co-design through delivery through rigorous evaluation and iteration. Does not cover: individualized case management for community members in crisis (GPS 6.0), physical resources that programs may include (GPS 4.0), or flourishing-specific program design requirements (GPS 15.2).
5.1
Design programs and services with community
Co-design processes through which programs are developed with the participation of community members who will use them — applying human-centered design principles within a solidarity economy values framework.
5.1.1
Conduct community needs assessment — systematic, participatory assessment grounding program design in documented evidence
5.1.1.1
Design needs assessment process with community member participation in data collection and interpretation
5.1.1.2
Disaggregate needs assessment findings by demographic subgroup to reveal differential need patterns
5.1.1.3
Update community needs assessment at minimum every three years or when community conditions change significantly
5.1.2
Apply the seven program design principles — trauma-informed, culturally responsive, structurally aware, participant-led, evidence-informed, dignifying, and transformative
5.1.2.1
Review each program design against all seven principles with community member participation in the review
5.1.2.2
Design program theory of change connecting activities to outcomes through explicit causal logic
5.1.3
Co-design program models with intended participants — ensuring those who will use programs have meaningful input into their design before launch
5.1.3.1
Convene participant design cohorts for new program development
5.1.3.2
Test program prototypes with representative participants before full launch
5.2
Develop and build participant capacity
Processes for building participants' skills, knowledge, and capacity as a primary program function — not as a byproduct of service receipt but as an intentional program design goal.
5.2.1
Design capacity-building curriculum with explicit learning objectives and adult learning principles
5.2.1.1
Develop curriculum grounded in participant knowledge and experience as well as institutional expertise
5.2.1.2
Design assessment mechanisms that track learning progress without replicating punitive educational models
5.2.2
Build civic and leadership capacity as a program function — developing participants' capacity for governance participation and community leadership
5.2.2.1
Include civic education and leadership development in program curriculum where mission-appropriate
5.2.2.2
Create pathways from program participation to organizational governance participation
5.3
Deliver direct services with quality and accountability
Processes for day-to-day delivery of institutional services — managing program quality, staff performance, participant experience, and service continuity across the full service delivery cycle.
5.3.1
Implement program quality standards and monitoring — defining what quality looks like for each program and monitoring delivery against those standards
5.3.1.1
Develop quality standards for each program co-designed with participants and staff
5.3.1.2
Conduct regular program observation and participant feedback collection during delivery
5.3.2
Manage service continuity and disruption response
5.3.2.1
Develop service continuity plans for each major program covering staffing, facility, and supply disruptions
5.3.2.2
Communicate service disruptions to participants proactively with alternative arrangements
5.3.3
Manage participant feedback infrastructure — formal mechanisms through which participants provide input on service quality during delivery
5.3.3.1
Implement mid-program feedback collection with governance body review and staff response protocols
5.3.3.2
Close the feedback loop — communicate to participants how their feedback was acted on
5.4
Support economic empowerment and financial wellbeing
Processes for programs specifically addressing economic dimensions of community member flourishing — income, assets, financial capability, and economic self-determination.
5.4.1
Design economic empowerment programming — programs that build economic agency and self-determination rather than only addressing immediate economic deficits
5.4.1.1
Develop financial capability curriculum addressing budget management, credit, and asset building
5.4.1.2
Connect participants to worker cooperative, small business, and solidarity economy economic pathways
5.4.2
Address structural economic barriers — connecting program delivery to advocacy for necessary policy changes
5.4.2.1
Document structural barriers encountered by program participants and feed documentation into GPS 12.4 policy advocacy
5.5
Evaluate programs rigorously and iterate based on findings
Processes for designing, conducting, and acting on program evaluation — distinguishing rigorous evaluation from activity reporting, and learning-oriented evaluation from compliance documentation.
5.5.1
Design evaluation frameworks aligned with program theories of change — measuring outcomes the program is designed to produce, not only outputs easy to count
5.5.1.1

How to use this reference in practice: Begin with the Quick-Reference Summary Table to orient to the category structure. Use the category scope statements to confirm which category governs a specific institutional process. Navigate the process group hierarchy to locate the specific process or activity. Use the Key Interfaces row to identify related GPS categories that should be developed in parallel. Use the Cross-Reference Index to locate the chapter where the process group is discussed in depth, including its characteristic failure modes, maturity indicators, KPIs, and development sequence.

On the completeness of the L4 activities: The activities listed for each process are representative rather than exhaustive — they describe the primary activities that constitute the process rather than every possible activity that could be included. Institutions adapting the GPS framework to their specific context should treat the L4 activities as a starting point for developing their own institution-specific activity lists. The process group and process level (L2 and L3) should be treated as the stable reference; the activity level (L4) is the most context-specific and appropriately variable across institutional forms, communities, and operational scales.
