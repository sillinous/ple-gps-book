Govern through citizen assemblies, community juries, and deliberative panels
Processes for convening representative samples of community members for extended deliberation on significant institutional questions — providing the depth and breadth of engagement that neither representative governance bodies nor open public comment can achieve.
16.3.1
Design and convene citizen assemblies for major strategic decisions — randomly or stratified-randomly selected panels with genuine advisory or binding authority
16.3.1.1
Design participant selection methodology ensuring demographic representation of the community served
16.3.1.2
Develop information provision protocol presenting balanced perspectives from multiple sources
16.3.1.3
Specify in advance whether assembly output is binding, strongly advisory, or advisory and communicate this to participants and community
16.3.1.4
Compensate assembly participants for their time at a rate that makes participation accessible regardless of income
16.3.2
Develop community jury processes for specific contested decisions — smaller deliberative panels hearing testimony and producing recommendations
16.3.2.1
Design jury process for contested institutional decisions (8–25 members, structured testimony, defined deliberation period)
16.3.2.2
Document governance body's formal response to jury recommendations within defined timeframe
16.3.3

16.3.3.1
Engage professional facilitators with community governance experience for all citizen assemblies and juries
16.3.3.2
Design facilitation protocols that specifically counteract dominant-voice dynamics and ensure equitable participation
16.3.4
Connect deliberative panel outputs to institutional governance — formal relationship between panel recommendations and governance body decisions with accountability for departures
16.3.4.1
Design formal protocol for governance body response to citizen assembly recommendations

16.5
Govern power distribution and democratic accountability across the institution
Processes for making power distribution visible, democratically legitimate, and continuously examined — including power mapping, structural power redistribution, democratic accountability mechanisms, and governance succession that broadens rather than concentrates authority.
16.5.1
Conduct power mapping — systematic analysis of where formal and informal power resides in the institution, distinguishing actual influence from formal authority
16.5.1.1
Design power mapping process involving community members, staff, and governance members as co-analysts rather than only subjects
16.5.1.2
Map formal decision authority alongside informal influence networks, funder relationships, and staff expertise authority
16.5.1.3
Conduct power mapping annually and compare year-over-year to assess power distribution trends
16.5.2
Design and implement power redistribution mechanisms — specific governance changes that address power concentration revealed by mapping
16.5.2.1
Develop power redistribution plan from power mapping findings with time-bound governance commitments
16.5.2.2
Design structural redistribution (new governance roles, expanded decision scope, community veto rights) rather than only cultural aspirations
16.5.2.3
Track power distribution index year-over-year as a governance performance indicator
16.5.3
Implement democratic accountability mechanisms — processes through which governance authority holders are accountable to community members between elections and without formal grievance processes
16.5.3.1
Design regular accountability sessions between governance members and their constituencies
16.5.3.2
Implement recall or removal mechanisms for governance members who fail community accountability standards
16.5.3.3
Design governance authority as a community mandate (mandar obedeciendo) rather than personal power through short terms, rotation, and return to ordinary membership
16.5.4
Govern the governance — regular community assessment of whether participatory democracy processes are producing genuine community agency
16.5.4.1
Survey community members annually on civic efficacy — whether their participation influences institutional decisions
16.5.4.2
Present governance quality assessment to governance body with accountability for improving participation spectrum level
16.5.4.3
Assess participation spectrum position for each major governance domain annually
16.5.5
Manage governance succession toward broader authority distribution — designing transitions that widen rather than replicate existing power concentrations
16.5.5.1
Assess governance succession plans against power distribution impact — succession that replicates existing demographics and networks is not neutral
16.5.5.2
Design succession processes that deliberately recruit from underrepresented community constituencies
16.5.5.3
Document governance authority transfer and assess whether each transition broadened or narrowed the distribution of institutional power

End of Appendix A — GPS Process Taxonomy Reference
Sixteen categories. Eighty process groups. Approximately 340 processes. Approximately 1,400 activities.
How to use this reference in practice: Begin with the Quick-Reference Summary Table to orient to the category structure. Use the category scope statements to confirm which category governs a specific institutional process. Navigate the process group hierarchy to locate the specific process or activity. Use the Key Interfaces row to identify related GPS categories that should be developed in parallel. Use the Cross-Reference Index to locate the chapter where the process group is discussed in depth, including its characteristic failure modes, maturity indicators, KPIs, and development sequence.

On the completeness of the L4 activities: The activities listed for each process are representative rather than exhaustive — they describe the primary activities that constitute the process rather than every possible activity that could be included. Institutions adapting the GPS framework to their specific context should treat the L4 activities as a starting point for developing their own institution-specific activity lists. The process group and process level (L2 and L3) should be treated as the stable reference; the activity level (L4) is the most context-specific and appropriately variable across institutional forms, communities, and operational scales.
