Chapter 13 · GPS Category 6.0

Support community members

When the institution meets the individual — designing direct support that sustains human dignity, prevents secondary harm, and maintains the humanity of both the people receiving help and the people providing it

Civic Operating Prosperity L1 Dignity by design 5 process groups 22 processes 15 KPIs

There is a moment, somewhere in every post-labor institution's work, when the scale falls away. The strategic plans and program portfolios and governance frameworks recede, and the institution is present with a single person in a specific situation — a family that has just lost housing, a member navigating a mental health crisis, a community elder with no one to call at three in the morning. What happens in those moments determines more about the institution's actual character than any document it has produced or any governance structure it has built. It is in those moments that the institution either is or is not what it says it is.

Category 6.0 is the process domain for those moments and for the systematic infrastructure that makes those moments go well consistently rather than depending on which staff member happens to be present and what they happen to know. It covers case management, grievance resolution, crisis response, peer support facilitation, and outcome tracking — the full operational territory of direct individual support. It is the category most resistant to systematization, because the human situations it addresses are irreducibly particular and because the people who do this work typically entered it out of deep personal commitment to the people they serve rather than out of interest in organizational process. And it is the category where the failure to systematize is most consequential — where the absence of processes produces outcomes that range from inadequate support to active institutional harm.

The central design challenge of Category 6.0 can be stated simply: how do you build systems that make direct human support consistent, accountable, and transmissible across staff transitions — without making it so systematized that it stops being human? This tension between consistency and humanization is not resolvable by choosing one over the other. The institutions that have done this work best have learned to systematize the framework while preserving the relationship — to use process to protect and sustain the human encounter rather than to replace it.

The systematization tension — what gets lost on each side

The design tension in Category 6.0 is real, and its resolution requires understanding what is lost when each side is overemphasized. Institutions that over-systematize direct support produce processes that are consistent and accountable but experienced as dehumanizing — as bureaucratic case processing rather than human encounter. Institutions that under-systematize produce support that is warm and relationship-based but inequitable, inconsistent, and entirely dependent on people who will eventually leave. Neither extreme serves community members well. The design work is in the middle: identifying which elements of direct support most benefit from systematization and which most depend on individual judgment, and designing accordingly.

What systematization protects
Consistency — community members receive comparable quality of support regardless of which staff member they encounter
Equity — support allocation is governed by explicit criteria rather than by personal relationships or staff preferences
Accountability — support processes can be examined, evaluated, and improved; outcomes can be tracked across the full caseload
Continuity — institutional knowledge about community members' situations survives staff transitions
Staff sustainability — clear processes prevent the accumulation of informal obligations that produce burnout
Legibility — community members can understand and navigate the support system, contest decisions they believe are wrong, and anticipate what will happen next
What over-systematization destroys
Dignity — community members' situations are irreducibly particular; systems that force them into standardized categories fail to respond to what is actually happening
Trust — the relationship between support provider and community member is the mechanism through which support often works; systems that eliminate relationship eliminate effectiveness
Flexibility — the situations that most require support are the ones that most resist categorization; rigid processes fail at the margins where need is most acute
Judgment — effective support requires the capacity to recognize when standard processes are inadequate and to respond differently; systems that eliminate judgment eliminate effectiveness
Meaning — for staff, the relationship with community members is often the primary source of professional meaning; over-systematization removes this and accelerates burnout
Agency — community members must remain active agents in their own situations, not passive recipients of prescribed support plans; systems that produce dependency undermine the wellbeing they aim to support

The resolution is not a compromise between the two columns but a design that honors both: systems that structure the framework while preserving the relationship, that create consistency in process without creating uniformity in encounter, and that build accountability for outcomes while protecting the judgment that produces those outcomes. The specific design principles that achieve this are addressed in the trauma-informed care framework below and operationalized in the process groups that follow.

Systematize the framework. Preserve the relationship. The system is there to support the encounter — not to replace it.

Trauma-informed care as institutional design — six principles operationalized

Trauma-informed care is the most rigorous framework available for designing direct support processes that are simultaneously consistent and human — that systematize appropriately without producing secondary harm through institutional interaction. Originally developed in mental health and healthcare contexts, it has been extended across social services, education, housing, and legal services as a design philosophy for any institution that works with people whose histories include adverse experiences with institutional actors. In post-labor contexts, trauma-informed design is not optional: the communities most in need of institutional support are typically those with the most sustained histories of institutional harm. Designing support without attending to those histories produces institutions that re-traumatize the people they are trying to help.

The six principles of trauma-informed care — safety, trustworthiness, peer support, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural sensitivity — are not values to be stated but operational requirements to be designed into processes. Each has specific implications for how Category 6.0 processes should be structured.

Trauma-informed care — six principles as Category 6.0 process design requirements
Safety
Physical and psychological safety for community members in all support interactions — including physical space design, confidentiality assurances, and the elimination of institutional interactions that activate threat responses. Safety is not merely the absence of physical danger; it is the presence of conditions in which people can be honest about their situations without fear of judgment, punishment, or loss of support.
Process implications: Confidentiality protocols with clear limits communicated in advance; intake environments designed for privacy; no mandatory reporting requirements that are not disclosed at first contact; staff trained in de-escalation rather than compliance enforcement.
Trustworthiness
Transparency about processes, decisions, and the institution's limits — so that community members can develop accurate expectations rather than experiencing institutional behavior as unpredictable or arbitrary. Trustworthiness is built through consistency between what the institution says it will do and what it actually does, including consistency in acknowledging when it has failed to do what it said.
Process implications: Clear explanation of what support is available and what is not, communicated without minimization; documented follow-through on commitments; explicit disclosure of mandatory reporting requirements and other limits on confidentiality; honest communication about case status and decisions, including decisions that community members will find difficult.
Peer support
Inclusion of people with lived experience of the situations being addressed as core support providers — not as adjuncts to professional services but as primary relationship-holders whose experience is the basis of their effectiveness. Peer support workers can provide what professional workers without that experience cannot: the relational credibility that comes from shared experience, and the practical knowledge of how systems actually work from the perspective of those who must navigate them.
Process implications: Peer support worker roles designed as institutional roles with compensation, training, supervision, and career pathways — not as volunteer positions or informal helpers; peer workers integrated into case management teams with clearly defined collaborative relationships with professional staff; peer worker wellbeing actively managed, including trauma-aware supervision for people doing emotionally demanding work from a position of shared vulnerability.
Collaboration
Power sharing between institutional staff and community members in the design and delivery of support — recognizing that community members are experts on their own situations and that effective support plans are co-developed rather than prescribed. Collaboration does not mean that community members must make all decisions; it means that their knowledge, preferences, and judgment are treated as primary inputs rather than secondary considerations.
Process implications: Support plans developed with community members, not for them — community members present, contributing, and retaining agency over plan elements that affect their lives; regular plan review meetings structured as collaborative reassessments rather than compliance checks; community members' disagreement with plan elements documented and, where possible, accommodated rather than overridden.
Empowerment
Designing support to build rather than replace community members' own capacity — ensuring that institutional support is a bridge to self-determination rather than a permanent dependency. Empowerment is not motivational rhetoric; it is a design requirement: support processes that create dependency rather than building capacity are not doing what they are meant to do, regardless of how much care goes into delivering them.
Process implications: Support goals explicitly include community members' own capacity development alongside crisis resolution; transition planning from institutional support to self-managed or peer-managed wellbeing built into every case from the beginning; progress toward independence tracked alongside stability outcomes; community members recognized as resources for other community members, not only as recipients of institutional resources.
Cultural sensitivity
Recognition of the specific cultural, historical, and community contexts that shape community members' experiences of institutions, their understanding of help and support, and their capacity to trust institutional actors. Cultural sensitivity is not a surface adaptation of institutional processes for different audiences; it is the recognition that the dominant institutional culture of most support organizations is itself a culture — with specific assumptions about help, autonomy, family, and institutional relationship that are not universally shared and may actively conflict with the values and practices of the communities being served.
Process implications: Cultural competency integrated into staff hiring, training, and supervision — not as a discrete training but as an ongoing organizational capacity; service co-design with community members whose cultural backgrounds differ from the institutional founding culture; community-specific historical awareness in case management practice, including awareness of the specific institutional actors whose actions have produced the trust deficits that community members bring to institutional encounters.
The five process groups — in depth
Category 6.0 — process groups and L4 activities
6.1
Provide direct member support and case management
Case management is the primary process through which institutions provide individualized support to community members navigating complex situations — situations where needs are multiple, interconnected, and evolving over time in ways that require coordinated institutional response rather than isolated program participation. The case management relationship is, at its best, a sustained partnership between a staff member and a community member that provides continuity, coordination, advocacy, and accountability across the community member's full situation. At its worst, it is a documentation process that generates records of institutional activity while providing minimal support to the individual whose name appears in the records.
6.1.1
Develop case management protocols that specify intake process, needs assessment methodology, plan development, progress monitoring, and case closure — with community member participation at each stage
6.1.2
Implement a case management information system that maintains continuity of case knowledge across staff transitions — so that no community member must start over because their case manager changed
6.1.3
Design caseload management standards — maximum caseloads per case manager that allow genuine relationship rather than administrative processing; indicators for caseload adjustment when community member complexity increases
6.1.4
Build internal referral and warm handoff protocols — systematic processes for connecting community members to additional institutional services or external resources, with documented responsibility for follow-up
6.1.5
Implement case supervision — regular review of active cases by supervisors, designed to support case managers in difficult situations rather than only to audit for compliance
6.1.6
Track case outcomes and caseload-level patterns — identifying systemic issues that emerge across multiple cases and require program-level rather than case-level response
Caseload management standards in 6.1.3 are the most commonly ignored process requirement in case management design — and their absence is the primary structural driver of case manager burnout. Case management organizations routinely assign caseloads that preclude genuine relationship: a case manager with 60 active cases cannot maintain meaningful connection with any of them, and the resulting work is administrative processing that satisfies institutional recording requirements while providing inadequate support. Designing and enforcing maximum caseload standards requires resource commitments that institutions consistently resist making, while the cost of their absence — in staff turnover, community member outcomes, and institutional effectiveness — is consistently underestimated.
6.2
Manage grievances, appeals, and conflict resolution
Grievance management is the institutional accountability mechanism closest to community members — the process through which individuals who believe the institution has acted unjustly can seek redress. It is also the process most commonly designed to protect the institution rather than the community member: grievance systems in most social service and solidarity economy institutions are formally available but practically inaccessible, producing records of institutional compliance rather than genuine accountability. A genuinely functional grievance system is one that community members can actually use, that produces findings that the institution genuinely acts on, and that over time changes institutional behavior in ways that reduce the grievances that created it.
6.2.1
Design a grievance intake process that is accessible without institutional navigation skills — the person who most needs to file a grievance is often the one least able to navigate complex intake procedures
6.2.2
Establish independent grievance review — review panels or processes that include independence from the staff whose decisions are being challenged; conflict-of-interest management that is genuine rather than nominal
6.2.3
Provide grievance navigation support — assistance for community members who want to file grievances but need support through the process; ideally provided by peer advocates rather than institutional staff
6.2.4
Implement timely response standards — maximum resolution timeframes that are actually enforced; escalation procedures for unresolved grievances
6.2.5
Analyze grievance patterns — regular review of resolved grievances for systemic issues that require process-level rather than case-level correction; this analysis is the governance mechanism through which grievance data becomes institutional improvement
6.2.6
Manage community member conflicts — processes for resolving disputes between community members rather than only between community members and the institution; peer mediation capacity development
Process 6.2.5 — analyzing grievance patterns — is the process that converts grievance management from a legal compliance function into a quality improvement function. Institutions that resolve individual grievances without examining the patterns across them are satisfying individual community members while leaving the institutional practices that generated their grievances intact. Quarterly governance review of grievance pattern data — asking "what is the institution doing consistently that generates these complaints, and what must change?" — is the accountability mechanism that makes grievance management consequential rather than procedural.
6.3
Deliver wraparound and crisis support services
Wraparound support is the response to the reality that community members' most difficult situations rarely have a single-domain cause or a single-domain solution. Housing instability is accompanied by economic insecurity and mental health stress and family relationship strain. The single-service model — providing one type of support and expecting it to address the full situation — consistently underperforms because the supporting conditions for its effectiveness are absent. Wraparound support coordinates multiple service responses around a single individual's needs, managing the integration that community members cannot manage for themselves when their situations are most difficult. Crisis response is the most acute version of this: when a situation becomes immediately threatening, the institution must have the capacity to respond quickly, effectively, and without compounding the harm through institutional interaction.
6.3.1
Develop crisis response protocols — tiered response levels, clear decision criteria for escalation, designated crisis response roles, and coordination with external emergency services — designed before crisis occurs, not in the moment of one
6.3.2
Build 24/7 crisis support capacity — on-call staff or peer support availability for community members experiencing acute situations outside business hours; crisis situations do not observe institutional schedules
6.3.3
Design and implement wraparound coordination processes — multi-service coordination meetings, shared care planning, and the inter-service communication protocols that prevent service fragmentation
6.3.4
Develop safety planning processes for community members in high-risk situations — collaborative development of concrete plans for managing risk, with community member agency at the center
6.3.5
Manage critical incident response — structured processes for responding to and learning from serious adverse events involving community members, including documentation, support for affected staff, and systemic review
Process 6.3.2 — 24/7 crisis support capacity — is among the most resource-intensive process requirements in Category 6.0 and among the most consequential. Crises that occur outside business hours — which is when many of the most acute situations arise — met with institutional voicemail produce outcomes that range from community member harm to institutional liability. Developing after-hours capacity does not necessarily require 24/7 staffing: peer support networks, on-call rotation systems, partnerships with crisis hotlines, and clearly communicated community member access to peer support can collectively address after-hours need without requiring round-the-clock institutional staffing. The design question is what coverage is appropriate for the specific community and what combination of mechanisms provides it.
6.4
Facilitate peer support networks and mutual aid within the community
Peer support is not a substitute for professional support — it is a distinct and often more effective form of support for specific kinds of need. The person who has lived through a housing crisis can offer something to someone in a current housing crisis that no professionally trained housing counselor can: the credibility of shared experience, the practical knowledge of how systems actually feel to navigate from the inside, and the relationship of equals rather than the relationship of helper and helped. Category 6.4 develops the institutional processes for building and sustaining peer support networks within the community — not as informal arrangements dependent on individual goodwill but as designed institutional infrastructure with training, supervision, compensation where possible, and the organizational support that makes sustained peer support viable.
6.4.1
Design and implement peer support worker programs — recruitment from the community, training in peer support methodology, supervision structures that support peer workers' own wellbeing, and career pathways that recognize peer support as professional work
6.4.2
Facilitate peer support groups — structured, facilitated groups where community members with shared experiences support each other, with institutional infrastructure (space, facilitation training, coordination) but community member leadership
6.4.3
Build mutual aid infrastructure within the community — the practices and platforms through which community members support each other directly, with the institution providing infrastructure rather than mediating every exchange
6.4.4
Support peer advocacy development — building community members' capacity to advocate for each other and for themselves in systems they must navigate, reducing long-term dependence on institutional advocacy
6.4.5
Manage peer worker wellbeing — trauma-aware supervision and support for peer workers who are providing support from a position of shared vulnerability; secondary trauma and burnout prevention specific to peer support roles
6.5
Track and report on individual member outcomes and wellbeing
Individual outcome tracking in Category 6.0 serves two distinct functions: it provides the case manager and community member with information needed for effective plan management, and it aggregates across cases to provide the institution with information about whether its support processes are producing the community outcomes it intends. Both functions require outcome measurement that goes beyond service delivery counting — that captures changes in community members' actual situations rather than only the support interactions the institution has provided. The tension between these two functions is that measurement designed for aggregate institutional learning can feel intrusive or surveillance-like at the individual level; measurement designed for individual case management may not aggregate in ways that produce institutional intelligence. Designing for both requires explicit attention to how measurement serves community members as well as institutional learning.
6.5.1
Develop individual outcome frameworks that measure change in community members' situations across multiple wellbeing domains — not only the specific needs that occasioned institutional contact but the broader conditions of community members' lives
6.5.2
Implement measurement practices that are useful to community members — outcome data collected in ways that support community members' own reflection on progress and goal-setting, not only institutional recording requirements
6.5.3
Track outcomes longitudinally — beyond case closure, maintaining contact with former community members to assess whether support produced durable change or temporary stabilization
6.5.4
Aggregate individual outcomes to caseload-level and institution-level analysis — identifying patterns in outcomes across cases that reveal systemic support gaps rather than only individual variations
6.5.5
Protect community member data — consent-based data collection, data minimization, secure storage, and clear policies about who can access case information and for what purposes; data governance that prioritizes community member privacy over institutional convenience
Process 6.5.2 — implementing measurement practices that are useful to community members — is the process most commonly absent in outcome tracking systems designed for funders rather than for the people being served. Outcome measurement tools designed as institutional accountability instruments typically produce data that funders can analyze and community members have no use for. Measurement tools designed to support community members' own goal-setting and progress reflection — visual tracking tools, simple check-in formats, collaborative review processes — produce data that community members find useful and that, as a secondary benefit, can be aggregated for institutional reporting. Designing measurement to serve community members first typically produces better institutional data as a result, because community members engage more honestly with instruments they experience as useful rather than intrusive.
Six direct support failure modes
Characteristic failures in direct member support and case management
C1
Throughput case management — processing people rather than supporting them
Case management is designed and resourced for high volume with low depth: many cases per case manager, short contact times, standardized plans applied across diverse situations, and documentation as the primary output. Community members are processed through the system efficiently; their situations are inadequately understood, their plans are inadequately individualized, and the institutional record of their cases is far more complete than the institutional support they received.
Early signal: Case managers carry caseloads that make genuine relationship mathematically impossible. Case plans are similar across diverse situations. Case manager turnover is high and attributed to stress; the connection between caseload and burnout is acknowledged but not addressed through caseload management. Community members describe feeling "like a file number."
C2
Inaccessible grievance — the right to complain without the ability to
Grievance procedures are formally documented and legally compliant but practically inaccessible to community members who lack institutional navigation skills, literacy, or the willingness to risk their relationship with a service they depend on. The institution can demonstrate that grievance procedures exist; it cannot demonstrate that they are actually used or that they produce outcomes proportional to the legitimate grievances in the community.
Early signal: Grievance filing rates are very low relative to the scale and complexity of services provided. Community members describe knowing the grievance process exists but not knowing how to use it. Staff are uncertain about the grievance process and unable to explain it to community members who ask. The institution has never received a grievance about a decision later found to be unjust.
C3
Crisis unprepared — designing response after the crisis arrives
The institution has no documented crisis response protocols. When crisis situations arise — as they do in any institution working with people under significant stress — response is improvised based on whoever happens to be present and whatever they happen to know. Improvised response is inconsistent, produces secondary harms through institutional interaction, and fails to draw on the institutional learning that documented protocols encode.
Early signal: Staff cannot describe the crisis response protocol when asked. Different staff describe different procedures for the same crisis type. The institution has no documented protocols for specific crisis types it regularly encounters. Critical incidents produce ad hoc response and after-action discussion but no protocol development for the next occurrence.
C4
Dependency by design — support that prevents rather than builds capacity
Support processes are designed to deliver assistance but not to build community members' capacity to manage their own situations — producing relationships in which community members remain dependent on institutional support for situations they could, with appropriate investment, learn to manage independently. The dependency is often experienced by staff as evidence of ongoing need rather than as evidence of inadequate empowerment design, making it self-reinforcing.
Early signal: Support plans do not include explicit capacity-building goals alongside stabilization goals. Community members' length of involvement with the institution increases over time without a corresponding increase in their independent functioning. Community members who exit support programs return at high rates with the same presenting situations. Staff describe community members as "not ready for independence" without examining what the institution has done to build the preconditions for independence.
C5
Secondary harm — institutional interaction that compounds the original difficulty
The institution's support interactions produce harm alongside or instead of help — through re-traumatizing community members whose histories include institutional harm, through disrespectful or dismissive encounters that confirm negative expectations about institutions, through privacy violations that expose sensitive information without consent, or through well-intentioned interventions that undermine community members' agency and dignity. Secondary harm is often invisible to the institution because the community members who experience it often do not return to report it.
Early signal: Community members disengage from services after initial contact at higher rates than can be explained by need resolution. Staff report difficult or tense encounters but attribute tension to community members' resistance rather than to institutional interaction design. No trauma-informed training or design review has been conducted. Community members from specific demographic groups disengage at substantially higher rates than others — a pattern that often reflects differential secondary harm across groups with different institutional histories.
C6
Staff sacrifice — sustaining support by consuming the people providing it
The institution sustains its direct support capacity by relying on staff members' personal commitment to absorb workloads, emotional demands, and institutional inadequacies that the institution has not addressed structurally. Staff burnout is high, turnover is frequent, and institutional knowledge is lost at every departure. The sustainability of the support is achieved at the cost of the wellbeing of those providing it — which is both ethically unacceptable and operationally counterproductive, because burned-out staff provide worse support than well-supported staff.
Early signal: Staff turnover in direct support roles is significantly higher than in other institutional roles. Departing staff cite stress and emotional exhaustion rather than compensation or career advancement as primary reasons for leaving. No structural response to turnover has been developed — each departure is treated as an individual rather than an institutional event. Peer workers and community-member staff are provided with no more support or lower caseloads than professional staff despite carrying additional burden from shared vulnerability.
Case study — Denver Basic Income Project and the limits of cash alone
Case study Denver Basic Income Project — when cash transfer needs Category 6.0
The Denver Basic Income Project, referenced in Chapter 10 for its access design innovations, provides an equally instructive Category 6.0 case study. DBIP's central design premise — that direct cash transfers to people experiencing homelessness would produce better outcomes than service-heavy case management — was validated by its outcome data: participants receiving larger cash amounts experienced greater improvements in housing stability and wellbeing than those receiving smaller amounts or services alone. Cash worked, often better than comparable investments in professional services.
What DBIP's experience also demonstrated is that cash alone — without the wraparound support processes of Category 6.0 — consistently underperformed cash-plus-support. Participants navigating acute crises alongside their cash transfers — mental health emergencies, family disruptions, encounters with the legal system, landlord conflicts — needed the case management and crisis support infrastructure that Category 6.0 operationalizes. The peer navigators who provided that support (drawing on process group 6.4) were the highest-value element of the program's support design: they provided the trust-based relationship infrastructure that professional case managers could not replicate, while the professional case management infrastructure provided the coordination and institutional knowledge that peer relationships alone could not sustain.
DBIP's most significant Category 6.0 contribution was its approach to the dignity tension: the program was designed from the start to treat participants as competent adults with agency over their own situations — providing cash without conditions, support without surveillance, and navigation without coercion. This approach, which operationalizes the empowerment principle of trauma-informed care across the entire program design, produced participant engagement levels and program satisfaction scores significantly higher than comparable programs with more conditional, compliance-oriented support designs.
Category 6.0 lesson: DBIP's experience illustrates both the power and the limits of the resource delivery model. Cash transfers without case management support leave participants vulnerable to the crises that disrupt their ability to use resources effectively. Case management without cash leaves participants with support relationships but inadequate material conditions for the outcomes the support is designed to produce. Category 4.0 (resources) and Category 6.0 (support) are complements, not substitutes — and the dignity design principle that runs through both is the element that makes each effective.
Case study — Strong Families and the caseload standard
Case study Strong Families Fund — redefining what case management capacity actually requires
Strong Families Fund, a California-based family support initiative, conducted a systematic examination of the relationship between case manager caseload and community member outcomes — a question that most case management organizations avoid examining because the findings are operationally uncomfortable. The study tracked outcomes across case managers with caseloads ranging from 15 to 80 families per case manager, controlling for community member characteristics and case complexity. The findings were unambiguous: case managers with caseloads above 25-30 families produced outcomes that were statistically indistinguishable from no case management at all for the most complex family situations. The relationship was not gradual — there was a threshold effect, above which the marginal benefit of additional case manager time fell to near zero because the minimum time required for genuine relationship and effective plan management could not be achieved.
Strong Families used these findings to redesign their case management model with a maximum caseload of 25 families per full-time case manager — a standard that required substantially more case management capacity per family served and that consequently reduced the number of families the program could reach at comparable funding levels. The tension this created between depth of service and breadth of reach is the central resource allocation challenge of Category 6.0: serving more people with less effective support, or fewer people with genuinely effective support. Strong Families' governance chose depth, based on the evidence that breadth without depth produces neither equity nor impact.
Category 6.0 lesson: The caseload standard is not an operational preference — it is a design requirement with documented outcome implications. Institutions that set caseloads based on available funding rather than on the minimum required for effective practice are making a decision to provide ineffective support at scale rather than effective support to fewer people. Making that decision transparently — acknowledging that the caseload is above the effective threshold and accepting the outcome implications — is at least honest. Making it while asserting that the support is effective is not.

The institution either is or is not what it says it is in those moments when the scale falls away and it is present with a single person in a specific situation. Process is what makes the answer consistent!

Development sequence — building Category 6.0 from Level 1
Recommended development sequence — Category 6.0 from Level 1 to Level 3
  • 01
    Document current case management practice and assess the caseload
    Before building new systems, document what the institution currently does in direct support: how cases are taken on, how plans are developed, how progress is tracked, how staff are supervised, and what caseloads are. Compare current caseloads to evidence-based effective caseload thresholds for the specific case types served. This assessment will typically reveal that the institution is operating above the threshold at which effective relationship-based support is possible — and will require a resource and prioritization conversation about whether to serve fewer people more effectively or continue current reach with documented quality limitations.
    Level 2 milestone: Current case management practice documented; caseload assessment completed and compared to effectiveness thresholds; governance body has reviewed and made an explicit decision about caseload standards and their implications for program reach.
  • 02
    Implement a case management information system and protocol
    Create the infrastructure that makes case knowledge institutional rather than personal: a case management information system that maintains continuity across staff transitions, and documented protocols for intake, needs assessment, plan development, and case closure. The protocol does not need to be elaborate — a well-designed simple protocol is more useful than an elaborate one that staff cannot follow consistently. Start with the minimum that would allow a new case manager to pick up an active case without starting over from the beginning.
    Level 2 milestone: Case management information system implemented and actively used by all case managers; case management protocol documented and followed consistently; new case manager onboarding process developed that uses the information system rather than informal knowledge transfer.
  • 03
    Design and document crisis response protocols for the most common crisis types
    Identify the three to five crisis types that occur most frequently in the community the institution serves. For each, develop a documented response protocol: what constitutes a crisis of this type, who is responsible for responding, what the initial response looks like, what escalation thresholds exist, how external resources are activated, and how the situation is documented and reviewed afterward. Train all staff in these protocols. Establish a regular crisis protocol review process to update protocols based on actual crisis experience.
    Level 3 milestone: Crisis protocols documented for all major crisis types; all staff trained and able to describe protocols accurately; after-action review conducted for all critical incidents; at least one protocol updated based on incident learning.
  • 04
    Make the grievance process accessible and consequential
    Review the current grievance process from a community member's perspective: Can someone with limited institutional navigation skills actually use it? Is there support available for community members who want to file but need help? Is the review process genuinely independent? Is there a documented pattern analysis that produces systemic change? Redesign the elements that fail these tests. Establish a quarterly governance review of grievance patterns with documented institutional response to patterns identified.
    Level 3 milestone: Grievance process redesigned for accessibility; grievance navigator support available; quarterly governance review of grievance patterns established; at least one systemic process change made based on grievance pattern analysis.
  • 05
    Launch a peer support worker program
    Identify community members with lived experience of the situations the institution's case management addresses and who express interest in peer support work. Develop training for the peer support role — not professional counseling training but peer support methodology, boundaries, self-care, and the institution's specific context. Establish compensation (peer support is work, and unpaid peer work is unsustainable), supervision, and career pathways. Integrate peer workers into case management teams with clearly defined collaborative roles. Develop and implement a peer worker wellbeing infrastructure from day one, before burnout becomes visible.
    Level 3 milestone: Peer worker program operational with at least three active peer workers; training, compensation, supervision, and career pathways established; peer workers integrated into case management teams with documented collaborative process; peer worker wellbeing infrastructure in place and functioning.

On Category 6.0 and Category 15.0 (Human Flourishing): Category 6.0 is the most proximate relationship between the institution and individual community members' wellbeing. Category 15.0 is the institutional process for measuring and building flourishing at the community level. The connection is direct: Category 6.0 process 6.5 (individual outcome tracking) provides the disaggregated individual data that Category 15.0 aggregates into community-level flourishing assessment. The theory of change that connects them is that institutions that provide effective, dignified, empowering direct support to people are building the conditions for community flourishing — and that institutions that measure and cultivate flourishing at the community level are holding their direct support processes accountable to something larger than case resolution rates.

On mandatory reporting and trust: Many direct support contexts involve mandatory reporting requirements — legal obligations to report specific categories of information to government agencies, typically involving child welfare, elder abuse, or imminent danger situations. Mandatory reporting requirements create a structural tension with the trust and confidentiality principles of trauma-informed care. The resolution is not to eliminate mandatory reporting — these requirements exist for important protective reasons — but to be completely transparent about them with community members from the first contact, so that the institution's confidentiality limits are known in advance rather than discovered in crisis. Surprise mandatory reporting destroys trust in ways that disclosed mandatory reporting doesn't.

On the staff wellbeing imperative: The staff sustainability failure mode (C6) is unique in Category 6.0 because it is an ethical failure as well as an operational one. Institutions that sustain direct support capacity by consuming the people providing it are not only producing worse outcomes — they are exploiting the very commitment that brings people into direct support work. Building Category 6.0 without building staff wellbeing infrastructure is building the capacity to help community members on the backs of staff members. The ethical and operational imperatives align: effective direct support requires well-supported staff, and well-supported staff requires that the institution treat the sustainability of their wellbeing as a design requirement rather than an individual coping challenge.
