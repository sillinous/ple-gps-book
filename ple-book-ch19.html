Inter-institutional relations

No institution has ever changed a system alone — the partnership disciplines, network practices, and policy engagement strategies through which solidarity economy institutions multiply their impact beyond the boundaries of any single organization

Institutional Support Network infrastructure Collective power 5 process groups 21 processes 13 KPIs

The solidarity economy is not an aggregation of independent institutions. It is a network — or, more precisely, an aspiration toward a network that the actual relationship infrastructure of most solidarity economy institutions does not yet fully realize. Individual institutions address bounded community needs with bounded resources. The problems they are addressing are not bounded: structural unemployment, housing insecurity, food system inequity, racial wealth gaps, and the distributional consequences of technological displacement are systemic conditions that no single institution, however mature its internal processes, can address from within its own organizational boundary. The institutional boundaries that define Category 7.0 through 11.0 — the human development, technology, financial, asset, integrity, and equity processes of a single institution — are necessary and insufficient. Systemic problems require systemic responses, and systemic responses require the inter-institutional relationships through which individual organizations become network participants, movement actors, and collective power builders.

The solidarity economy networks that survived the 2008 financial crisis largely survived as networks — the organizations in the Bay Area Solidarity Economy network shared resources, referred members, and pooled technical assistance in ways that no individual organization could have managed alone. The ones that didn't have networks didn't all fail, but they had a much harder time. Category 12.0 is the process domain for the relationship infrastructure that converts a collection of independent institutions into something more consequential: a network of peers that shares knowledge and resources, a coalition of actors with shared policy goals, a movement with coordinated strategy and distributed capacity. This is not aspirational language — it is a process design specification. The question Category 12.0 answers is not "should post-labor institutions collaborate?" (the answer is obvious) but "what specific processes make collaboration disciplined enough to produce collective impact rather than consuming relationship energy without commensurate return?"

The Mondragon cooperative network survived 2008 largely because fifteen years of inter-cooperative solidarity agreements meant that when one cooperative faced a demand collapse, others absorbed displaced workers rather than dismissing them. No single cooperative could have built that buffer alone. The network made it possible. The practical challenge that most post-labor institutions face in Category 12.0 is the relationship investment problem: genuine inter-institutional relationships require sustained investment — staff time, leadership attention, governance commitment — that competes directly with the internal processes of Categories 7.0 through 11.0 and with the civic operating work of Categories 1.0 through 6.0. Institutions facing resource pressure routinely reduce inter-institutional relationship investment before reducing internal operations, because the return on relationship investment is diffuse and long-horizon while the cost is immediate and direct. This is individually rational and collectively counterproductive: the cumulative effect of every institution reducing inter-institutional investment during resource pressure is a solidarity economy that is weakest precisely when systemic conditions make collective response most necessary.

Relationship typology — the architecture of solidarity economy networks

Inter-institutional relationships exist at multiple scales and serve different strategic functions. The typology below maps the primary relationship types that post-labor institutions need to develop and maintain — from bilateral service partnerships through peer learning networks through political coalitions through government relationships — with honest assessment of what each requires to sustain, what it produces when it works, and what failure modes characterize each relationship type. No single institution needs to maintain all relationship types at full depth simultaneously; the question is which types are strategically most important given the institution's specific mission, scale, and political context.

The network logic — why systemic change requires collective infrastructure

The argument for Category 12.0 investment is ultimately a theory of change argument: post-labor institutions that remain confined to their organizational boundaries can improve conditions within those boundaries — serving more community members, delivering better outcomes, building more durable institutional infrastructure — without changing the conditions that produce the need for those services in the first place. Changing structural conditions requires collective action at a scale that individual institutions can't achieve alone. The network logic below maps the specific types of collective impact that require inter-institutional infrastructure and the GPS process groups that develop the capacity to pursue them.

Network logic — types of collective impact and the inter-institutional capacity they require
Knowledge amplification
Individual institutions generate knowledge from their operations — what works, what doesn't, and why — that is valuable to the entire solidarity economy if shared. Without network infrastructure for knowledge sharing, that knowledge remains institutional and depreciates with staff turnover. With network infrastructure, it becomes collective capital that compounds across the network.
Example: An institution that develops an effective trauma-informed intake protocol shares it through a peer learning network. Fifteen peer institutions implement adapted versions over two years, improving their intake quality. None of the fifteen would have invested in developing the protocol independently; the network multiplied the value of one institution's development investment across all fifteen.
GPS 12.3 — peer learning and knowledge exchange processes
Resource resilience
Individual institutions face financial and resource shocks — grant non-renewal, unexpected demand surges, facility failures — that would be survivable with short-term support from peer institutions but are institution-threatening without it. Network relationships that include mutual aid provisions convert individual institution risk into shared network risk that is more manageable in aggregate.
Example: An emergency food network's largest warehouse is damaged by flooding. Three peer organizations in the network have available storage and activate their mutual aid agreements, temporarily absorbing the damaged institution's storage and distribution functions while the facility is repaired. Without the mutual aid agreement, the institution would have halted distribution for weeks; with it, community members experience minimal service disruption.
GPS 12.2 — resource sharing and mutual aid agreements
Policy power
Individual institutions may have relationships with specific elected officials or agency staff, and those relationships have value. But policy influence at the scale required to change the structural conditions that produce institutional service needs requires collective voice — the demonstrated constituency that comes from a network speaking together rather than institutions speaking independently.
Example: A coalition of affordable housing institutions represents 40,000 community members across a metropolitan region. When state affordable housing funding is threatened, the coalition's coordinated testimony, media presence, and constituent mobilization carries significantly more weight than any single institution's advocacy. The housing funding is preserved; it would not have been without the coalition.
GPS 12.4 — coalition and advocacy infrastructure
Infrastructure efficiency
Individual institutions build and maintain operational infrastructure — technology platforms, financial systems, training programs, communications tools — at significant cost. When multiple institutions need similar infrastructure, building it collectively is more efficient than building it independently, and shared infrastructure produces network integration benefits that individually built infrastructure cannot.
Example: A network of worker cooperatives develops a shared back-office platform — payroll, accounting, HR compliance — that each cooperative accesses at a fraction of the cost of building equivalent capacity independently. The platform also enables network-level financial reporting that makes collective grant applications and network financial management possible.
GPS 12.1 — partnership development and shared infrastructure
Legitimacy amplification
Individual institutions have limited institutional credibility with funders, media, and government actors who are more likely to engage with networks than with individual organizations. Network membership and network-level endorsement amplifies individual institution credibility and opens relationships that would not be accessible to any single institution in the network.
Example: A new community land trust is founded by a team with strong community roots but no institutional track record. Its membership in the national Community Land Trust Network provides credibility with state housing finance agencies, connection to experienced peer institutions, and access to technical assistance that makes its early operations significantly more effective than they would be without the network relationship.
GPS 12.5 — movement building and sector development
The five process groups — in depth
Category 12.0 — process groups and L4 activities
12.1
Develop, govern, and manage strategic partnerships
Partnership development is the process through which institutions identify, establish, and sustain relationships with specific peer organizations that advance shared objectives — not all possible relationships with all possible peers, but the specific relationships that are worth the investment of institutional time, resources, and governance attention required to sustain them. The most common Category 12.1 failure is treating partnership as the default mode for all inter-institutional contact: a phone call from a neighboring organization becomes a partnership, a shared funder becomes a collaborative, and the institution accumulates nominal partnerships that consume relationship management capacity without producing commensurate value. Effective partnership development requires deliberate selection based on strategic alignment, operational compatibility, and mutual benefit — and deliberate non-partnership with organizations where the case for sustained relationship investment is weak relative to other priorities.
12.1.1
Develop a partnership strategy — explicitly mapping which relationship types the institution most needs to develop, with which categories of peer organizations, in service of which specific strategic objectives
12.1.2
Design and implement partnership governance — written agreements specifying shared objectives, roles and responsibilities, resource contributions, decision rights, conflict resolution processes, and exit conditions for significant partnerships
12.1.3
Manage the partnership portfolio — maintaining an active view of all current partnerships, their health, their value, and the investment they require; making explicit decisions about which partnerships to deepen, maintain, and let lapse
12.1.4
Build and maintain referral networks — the service partnership infrastructure that connects community members to the full range of services they need without requiring any single institution to provide all of them
12.1.5
Develop shared infrastructure with strategic partners — technology platforms, training programs, financial systems, and communications infrastructure that are more efficiently built and operated collectively than independently
12.1.6
Assess partnership value and equity — regular evaluation of whether partnerships are producing mutual benefit or asymmetric extraction; specific attention to power differentials between larger and smaller partner institutions
Process 12.1.6 — partnership equity assessment — is the most commonly avoided evaluation process in inter-institutional relationships, because the power differentials between larger and smaller partner institutions are real and produce the asymmetric dynamics that equity assessment is designed to surface. Partnerships between institutions of very different sizes typically impose higher administrative burden on the smaller institution (which must adapt to the larger institution's systems, timelines, and reporting requirements) while directing most of the strategic benefit to the larger institution (which gains service reach, community relationships, and reputational association). Making this dynamic visible — through honest mutual assessment rather than the diplomatic non-evaluation that characterizes most partnership reviews — is the prerequisite for redesigning the partnership toward genuine mutuality.
12.2
Build and maintain mutual aid and resource-sharing agreements
Resource-sharing agreements are the inter-institutional equivalent of individual mutual aid — the formalized practices through which institutions support each other during resource shocks, demand peaks, and operational disruptions. They are the specific implementation of the supply chain resilience infrastructure that Category 4.1 identified as essential for physical resource institutions and that applies equally across all resource domains: financial resources during cash flow crises, human resources during staffing peaks, physical resources during facility disruptions, and knowledge resources during organizational transitions. Resource sharing requires more governance formalization than the informal mutual support that characterizes good peer relationships, because formalized agreements survive leadership transitions and activate under conditions of stress when informal arrangements often dissolve.
12.2.1
Develop resource sharing agreements with peer institutions — formally specifying what resources are available for sharing, under what conditions, with what reciprocity obligations, and through what governance process
12.2.2
Establish emergency mutual aid protocols — the specific activation procedures for resource sharing during institutional crises; protocols that are practiced before crises rather than designed during them
12.2.3
Participate in inter-institutional resource pools — shared financial reserves, shared equipment libraries, shared human resource capacity that provide resilience to all participants at lower cost than individual reserve building alone
12.2.4
Manage reciprocity in resource sharing relationships — tracking resource flows across sharing relationships to ensure they are genuinely mutual rather than consistently one-directional; addressing imbalances before they create institutional resentment
12.3
Participate in and contribute to peer learning networks and practice communities
Peer learning networks are the knowledge commons infrastructure that converts individual institutional learning into collective movement learning — the inter-institutional equivalent of the individual learning culture described in Category 7.0. They require the same design attention: protected time, integration with institutional work, and genuine knowledge contribution as well as knowledge consumption. The failure mode of peer learning — one institution extracting knowledge from better-resourced peers without contributing its own learning — is the network-level equivalent of the knowledge hoarding failure mode that Category 2.4 addresses at the institutional level. Peer learning networks that function well produce network-level learning curves: the whole solidarity economy learns faster than any institution within it could learn alone.
12.3.1
Participate actively in peer learning networks — not only attending learning events but contributing institutional knowledge, sharing evaluation findings, and serving in facilitation or governance roles in networks the institution values
12.3.2
Develop and share practice frameworks, tools, and documentation — the specific knowledge contribution that individual institutions can make to collective movement learning; includes evaluation findings, process designs, governance frameworks, and program models
12.3.3
Host peer learning with other institutions — welcoming peer institutions for observation and learning from institutional practice; the relational knowledge sharing that formal documentation cannot fully capture
12.3.4
Build staff capacity for network participation — ensuring that inter-institutional engagement is a recognized and supported part of relevant staff roles, not an add-on to full workloads that gets dropped when pressure increases
12.3.5
Assess network learning return on investment — periodically evaluating whether the institution's peer network participation is producing learning returns commensurate with the time investment, and adjusting participation accordingly
Process 12.3.4 — building staff capacity for network participation — is the process that makes the difference between network engagement as a leadership activity and network engagement as an institutional capacity. When only the executive director participates in peer networks, the network relationships are leadership-dependent and do not survive leadership transitions. When staff at multiple levels have peer network relationships — program staff with peer program staff, finance staff with peer finance staff, governance members with peer governance members — the inter-institutional relationship infrastructure is woven into the institution's operational fabric rather than depending on a single relationship holder.
12.4
Build policy advocacy, government relations, and coalition infrastructure
Policy advocacy is the process through which post-labor institutions attempt to change the structural conditions — the laws, regulations, funding systems, and institutional frameworks — that shape the environment in which they operate and within which their community members live. It is the most direct response available to the theory of change limitation identified in Chapter 12 (GPS 5.0): individual-level interventions produce limited outcomes when structural barriers constrain what individual improvement can achieve. Changing those barriers requires policy change, and policy change in democratic systems requires organized constituent voice — which is what coalition-based advocacy produces at its most effective. Policy advocacy in post-labor institutions is not optional political activity; it is the strategic complement to program delivery that addresses structural conditions rather than only individual circumstances.
12.4.1
Develop a policy agenda — identifying the specific legislative, regulatory, and funding changes that would most improve the institutional operating environment and the community conditions being addressed
12.4.2
Build and participate in policy coalitions — with peer institutions, community organizations, and other stakeholders sharing relevant policy objectives; coalition governance that enables collective action without requiring unanimity on all positions
12.4.3
Develop and maintain government relationships — with elected officials, agency staff, and regulatory bodies at the local, state, and federal levels relevant to the institution's operations and community; relationships maintained between advocacy campaigns, not only activated during them
12.4.4
Manage regulatory compliance and government contracting — the day-to-day government relations that support institutional operations rather than only the advocacy relationships that change structural conditions
12.4.5
Develop constituent voice for policy advocacy — building community members' capacity and willingness to engage in policy processes on their own behalf, rather than substituting institutional advocacy for community voice
Process 12.4.5 — developing constituent voice — is the process that distinguishes advocacy by institutions on behalf of communities from advocacy by communities through institutional infrastructure. The former produces service delivery organizations that also advocate; the latter produces organizing institutions that support community members in shaping the conditions of their own lives. Both are valuable; the distinction matters because constituent voice-based advocacy is more durable, more legitimate, and more difficult to dismiss than institutional advocacy alone. Community members describing their own experiences and demanding their own policy changes carry political weight that institutional representatives speaking on their behalf cannot fully replicate — which is why the best policy advocacy organizations invest heavily in developing community members' civic capacity rather than primarily in developing their own institutional advocacy capacity.
12.5
Contribute to solidarity economy movement building and sector development
Movement building is the long-horizon investment in the social infrastructure — the shared narrative, the visible identity, the connected network, the policy agenda — that makes the solidarity economy a recognized and politically potent sector rather than a collection of independent organizations sharing similar values but without the connective tissue that converts shared values into collective power. It is also the most diffuse and hardest-to-resource activity in Category 12.0: movement building produces returns that are systemic, long-horizon, and shared across all participants — the classic conditions for under-investment relative to the collective benefit it produces. Institutions that invest in movement building are making a commitment to the solidarity economy's long-term viability that market logic would not produce and that each institution must make as a deliberate governance choice.
12.5.1
Participate in solidarity economy sector bodies — membership and governance participation in networks, federations, and associations that build the institutional infrastructure of the solidarity economy as a sector
12.5.2
Contribute to shared narrative and communications — the collective story of what the solidarity economy is, what it does, and why it matters; shared across institutions to build the public awareness and political legitimacy that individual institutional communications cannot build alone
12.5.3
Support emerging institutions — providing mentorship, technical assistance, and network access to institutions in their early development stages; investing in the growth of the solidarity economy ecosystem as an institutional commitment
12.5.4
Contribute to solidarity economy research and documentation — supporting the research and knowledge development that builds the evidence base for solidarity economy approaches; sharing institutional data and experience for research purposes
12.5.5
Participate in solidarity economy ecosystem development — CDFIs, community investment funds, shared technology platforms, legal infrastructure, and the other shared resources that all solidarity economy institutions benefit from and that none can build independently
Six inter-institutional failure modes
Characteristic failures in inter-institutional relationships and network engagement
N1
Partnership inflation — nominal collaboration without value
The institution has accumulated a large number of nominal partnerships — MOU signatories, coalition members, network participants — whose practical effect on institutional operations and community outcomes is negligible. Partnership management consumes staff time without producing commensurate value. The institution cannot describe what specific outcomes any given partnership has produced. The partnership portfolio exists to demonstrate collaborative intentions rather than to produce collaborative impact.
Early signal: Staff cannot describe specific community outcomes attributable to specific partnerships. The institution has never let a partnership lapse despite low value, because doing so feels like a statement about the relationship. Partnership reviews do not distinguish between productive and nominal relationships. The number of listed partnerships on the institutional website far exceeds the partnerships in which staff have active working relationships.
N2
Asymmetric collaboration — the extractive partnership
Partnership relationships between institutions of different sizes systematically extract value from smaller, less resourced institutions while directing most of the benefit to larger, better-resourced ones. The smaller institution provides community relationships, trust, and local credibility; the larger institution provides resources, legitimacy, and scale but imposes administrative burden, decision timelines, and reporting requirements designed for its own internal systems rather than the smaller partner's capacity. The partnership is described as collaborative; it functions as a subcontract with relational language.
Early signal: Staff at the smaller institution describe feeling like "junior partners" despite nominally equal status. Administrative burden of the partnership is disproportionately borne by the smaller institution. Decision-making timelines are determined by the larger institution's governance cycles without accommodation for the smaller institution's. Staff time consumed by the partnership at the smaller institution significantly exceeds the value received.
N3
Competitive isolation — institutions that treat peers as rivals
The institution treats peer organizations in similar service domains as competitors — for funding, for community members, for media attention, for institutional legitimacy — rather than as potential collaborators in addressing shared problems. This orientation is partially rational (institutions do compete for restricted funding sources) but produces a systematic under-investment in the inter-institutional relationships that would benefit all participants, because the competitive frame makes collaboration feel strategically risky rather than strategically valuable.
Early signal: Staff describe peer organizations in service domains as competitors rather than collaborators. The institution does not share program evaluation data, outcomes evidence, or practice frameworks with peer organizations. Leadership decisions about participation in peer networks are filtered through competitive impact analysis. The institution's communications emphasize its distinctiveness from peer organizations rather than its common cause with them.
N4
Policy quietism — direct service without structural advocacy
The institution delivers effective direct services to its community while systematically avoiding policy advocacy on the structural conditions that produce the need for those services. The avoidance is rationalized as mission focus ("we're service providers, not advocates"), as funder risk management ("advocacy might jeopardize our government contracts"), or as resource scarcity ("we don't have the capacity for advocacy work"). The result is an institution that is effectively managing the symptoms of structural problems without contributing to the structural changes that would reduce those symptoms.
Early signal: The institution's staff can describe the structural conditions (policy failures, legal gaps, funding deficiencies) that produce the community needs it addresses but has no strategy for contributing to changing them. Policy advocacy is explicitly excluded from the institution's scope. Community members are not supported in civic engagement related to the conditions affecting their lives. No partnerships with advocacy-focused organizations exist.
N5
Network extraction — consuming movement infrastructure without contributing to it
The institution participates in solidarity economy networks, coalitions, and movement infrastructure as a consumer rather than as a contributor — accessing the collective resources, knowledge, legitimacy, and policy power that movement infrastructure provides without investing in sustaining that infrastructure. The behavior is individually rational (free riding is optimal from a narrow institutional perspective) and collectively destructive (movement infrastructure that is not sustained depletes). Institutions that consume movement infrastructure without contributing to it are free riding on the contributions of others in ways that, if generalized across the network, would destroy the infrastructure they depend on.
Early signal: The institution participates in solidarity economy networks and coalitions without holding governance or leadership roles. Staff do not contribute knowledge, tools, or practice frameworks to peer networks. The institution benefits from coalition policy wins without contributing to coalition advocacy capacity. Leadership describes movement participation as networking rather than as a strategic institutional obligation.
N6
Relationship fragility — inter-institutional relationships that collapse with leadership transitions
The institution's inter-institutional relationships are held by specific leadership figures rather than by the institution as a whole — dependent on the personal relationships of the executive director, key program staff, or governance members rather than on institutional relationships maintained through formal agreements, multi-level staff engagement, and documented relationship history. When those individuals depart, the relationships collapse, and the institution must rebuild its network from near-zero despite years of relationship investment by the previous leadership.
Early signal: Key partnership relationships are described primarily in terms of the personal connection between specific leaders. No formal partnership agreements or relationship documentation exists for significant inter-institutional relationships. New leadership describes needing to "start over" on external relationships. Staff-level peer relationships in partner organizations are absent or minimal.
Case study — US Federation of Worker Cooperatives and the sector-building investment
Case study US Federation of Worker Cooperatives — building the infrastructure of a sector
The US Federation of Worker Cooperatives (USFWC) is the primary national membership organization for worker cooperatives in the United States, providing the sector-building infrastructure — networking, peer learning, policy advocacy, and public narrative — that individual worker cooperatives cannot build independently. Its Category 12.0 contribution is specifically in operationalizing what process 12.5 describes as solidarity economy ecosystem development: the USFWC provides the shared infrastructure that makes the worker cooperative sector recognizable, navigable, and politically relevant rather than a collection of interesting but isolated enterprises.
The USFWC's most instructive Category 12.0 practices are in the area of member knowledge exchange and technical assistance. Its annual conference, its peer learning programming, and its Democracy at Work Institute training programs operationalize process 12.3 at sector scale — creating the systematic infrastructure through which individual worker cooperatives access knowledge they could not develop independently and contribute knowledge that raises the sector's collective capability. The USFWC's explicit policy that member institutions are contributors to as well as consumers of sector knowledge — operationalized through peer facilitation programs, member-developed resources, and governance roles open to member representatives — is the design mechanism that prevents the network extraction failure mode (N5) from characterizing member participation.
The USFWC's policy work demonstrates the coalition infrastructure described in process 12.4: its collaboration with state-level cooperative development organizations, labor unions, and community development institutions has produced enabling legislation for worker cooperatives in multiple states, favorable regulatory treatment in several federal programs, and the policy visibility that individual worker cooperatives could not achieve through their own advocacy. The policy returns are systemic and accrue to all member cooperatives, not only to those that contributed most heavily to the advocacy effort — the intended design of collective policy infrastructure.
Category 12.0 lesson: The USFWC's model demonstrates that sector-level infrastructure requires the same deliberate design, sustained investment, and governance discipline that individual institutional operations require. Sector infrastructure does not self-organize from shared values; it is built through specific governance decisions about what to fund, what to govern, what to share, and what to require of members. The USFWC's design choices — what technical assistance to offer, how to govern member knowledge contribution, which policy campaigns to pursue collectively — are Category 12.5 governance decisions that determine whether sector participation produces sector benefit or only sector identity.
Case study — Right to the City Alliance and coalition governance
Case study Right to the City Alliance — governing a coalition of diverse institutional actors
The Right to the City Alliance is a national coalition of urban community organizations and tenant associations working on housing, gentrification, and urban land rights — a coalition that must govern the collective action of member organizations with significantly different scales, governance structures, issue priorities, and political orientations while maintaining sufficient cohesion to pursue shared policy objectives. Its Category 12.0 contribution is specifically in the governance design of policy coalitions: how to coordinate collective action among peer institutions with shared values but divergent positions on many specific questions.
Right to the City's governance model operationalizes several elements of process 12.4.2 in ways that distinguish it from coalitions with nominally collaborative governance but practically concentrated decision-making. Its consensus decision-making model for collective positions — which requires each member organization to genuinely agree rather than simply not oppose — is slower than majority-rule governance and produces a more limited range of collective positions. It also produces more durable commitments: member organizations that have genuinely agreed to a position are more reliable advocates for it than organizations that were outvoted. The coalition's explicit autonomy protections — which affirm each member organization's right to pursue independent positions outside the coalition framework — reduce the governance cost of consensus by making the cost of disagreement lower.
Right to the City's approach to power analysis within the coalition is instructive for process 12.1.6 (partnership equity assessment): the coalition explicitly tracks participation, voice, and benefit across member organizations by organizational size and community demographics, and designs governance mechanisms to amplify the voice of smaller member organizations whose community constituencies are most directly affected by the coalition's policy agenda. This is the coalition-level operationalization of the equity audit framework described in Chapter 18.
Category 12.0 lesson: Right to the City's governance model demonstrates that coalition effectiveness requires explicit governance design that manages the power differentials between member organizations, not only shared commitment to common goals. Coalitions that assume power equity among members produce governance that is formally equal and practically dominated by the largest and best-resourced members. Coalitions that explicitly design for power equity — through voice amplification mechanisms, weighted governance, and equity auditing of coalition participation patterns — produce more genuinely collective decision-making and more durable commitments from members whose concerns are reflected in coalition positions.

No institution has ever changed a system alone. The work inside the institution matters. The relationships between institutions matter more for structural change — and they require the same deliberate design as any other process the institution builds.

  • 02
    Formalize the most important service partnerships with written agreements
    Select the three to five partnerships most important for community service continuity — the organizations to which the institution most frequently refers community members and from which it most frequently receives referrals. Develop written partnership agreements for each: shared understanding of each organization's services and eligibility criteria, warm handoff protocols, data sharing provisions where needed, and a regular relationship maintenance cadence. The agreements need not be elaborate — even a one-page memorandum of understanding with contact lists, referral procedures, and a commitment to quarterly check-ins dramatically outperforms the informal "we know each other" relationships that most service partnerships rest on.
    Level 2 milestone: Written partnership agreements for three to five primary service partnerships; warm handoff protocols implemented; referral tracking system operational; first annual partnership review conducted.
  • 03
    Join and actively participate in one peer learning network
    Select one peer learning network — a community of practice, a regional solidarity economy network, a national field-specific peer learning program — that is genuinely relevant to the institution's primary practice areas. Join with an explicit commitment to active participation: attending regularly, contributing knowledge as well as consuming it, and taking on a facilitation or governance role within 12 months. Build inter-network participation into a relevant staff member's role description so that it survives individual leadership transitions. The test of whether participation is genuine is whether the institution can name specific knowledge it has shared with the network and specific knowledge it has learned from it.
    Level 3 milestone: Active participation in one peer learning network sustained for 18 months; at least one knowledge contribution (tool, framework, evaluation finding, or training) made to the network; governance or facilitation role held; inter-network participation embedded in staff role description.
  • 04
    Develop resource-sharing agreements with the two closest peer institutions
    Identify the two peer institutions with the most operational overlap — similar service domains, similar geographic area, similar scale — and develop formal resource-sharing agreements covering the resource types most relevant to institutional resilience: emergency financial assistance, facility backup, human resource coverage during demand peaks, or supply chain resilience for physical resource institutions. Practice the activation of the agreements before they are needed urgently — a tabletop exercise of "what would we do if X happened?" with a peer institution is a cost-effective way to identify gaps in the agreements before those gaps become consequential.
    Level 3 milestone: Resource-sharing agreements with two peer institutions; at least one agreement exercise or activation documented; reciprocity balanced across agreements; emergency contact protocols current and tested.
  • 05
    Develop a policy agenda and join one policy coalition
    Develop a written policy agenda — the specific legislative, regulatory, or funding changes that would most improve the community conditions the institution addresses and the institutional operating environment — through a process that includes community member input on what structural changes they most want. Join one policy coalition whose agenda aligns with the institution's priorities and commit to active participation rather than nominal membership. Over time, develop community members' capacity to participate in advocacy on their own behalf rather than depending entirely on institutional representation. The policy agenda should be reviewed and updated annually as the regulatory and political environment evolves.
    Level 3 milestone: Policy agenda developed with community member input; active participation in one policy coalition with governance or facilitation role; at least one policy campaign in which the institution's participation was substantive; community member civic engagement program initiated.

On the relationship between Category 12.0 and Categories 5.0 and 16.0: Category 5.0 (Deliver Services and Programs) identified the structural barrier problem — the limit of what individual-level interventions can achieve when structural conditions constrain individual outcomes. Category 12.0's policy advocacy processes (12.4) are the institutional mechanism for addressing those structural barriers at the source rather than managing their consequences at the service level. The connection is explicit: institutions that have identified specific structural barriers in their program evaluation work (Category 5.5.6) have the most credible and specific basis for policy advocacy, because their advocacy is grounded in documented community experience rather than only in institutional position. Category 16.0 (Participatory Democracy) extends this connection further: community members who participate in institutional governance through Category 16.0 processes develop the civic skills and political consciousness that make them effective advocates for structural change through Category 12.4's constituent voice development processes.

On the ethics of collaboration in a competitive funding environment: The competitive funding environment — in which peer institutions compete for the same restricted grants, the same government contracts, and the same foundation priorities — creates genuine tensions with the collaborative orientation that Category 12.0 describes. These tensions are real and require active management rather than denial. Specific strategies for managing them: separate the competitive relationship (funding applications) from the collaborative relationship (service delivery, peer learning, advocacy), which are not inherently in conflict; develop the community-sourced and earned revenue that reduces competitive dependency on restricted grants; participate in collective advocacy for increased public investment in the solidarity economy as a sector, which grows the funding pool rather than only competing for the existing one. The most effective long-term response to the competitive funding dynamic is to change the funding environment through Category 12.4 policy advocacy rather than to accept it as a permanent constraint on inter-institutional collaboration.

On the movement relationship between institutional infrastructure and social movements: The solidarity economy institutions described throughout this framework are not social movements — they are institutional actors that provide services, manage assets, and operate within legal and regulatory frameworks. Social movements are collective efforts to change political and social conditions through organized constituent power rather than through institutional service delivery. The relationship between institutions and movements is critical and often poorly navigated: institutions that behave as though they are movements (substituting institutional positioning for constituent organizing) underperform as service institutions and overreach as movement actors. Movements that dismiss institutions as bureaucratic compromises of radical politics forgo the service capacity, legal standing, and resource management that institutions provide and that movements require to sustain long-term campaigns. Category 12.0's movement building processes (12.5) are designed for institutional actors that understand themselves as part of a broader movement ecosystem — providing specific institutional contributions (services, research, legal standing, financial infrastructure) to movement goals that they can't pursue alone but that the movement cannot achieve without them.
