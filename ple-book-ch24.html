Entering Part VI · Applied archetypes
Five institutional forms. Sixteen categories. The same framework assembles differently for each.
Parts I through V described the GPS framework as a whole — its categories, its maturity model, its process architecture. Part VI applies it. Each of the five archetypes that follow is a distinct institutional form with its own governance logic, its own financial structure, its own relationship to community power, and its own characteristic strengths and vulnerabilities. The GPS framework does not prescribe a single institutional model; it provides a shared process standard that all post-labor institutional forms can use to assess their current capabilities and navigate their development. What differs between archetypes is not the destination — mature, accountable, community-governed institutions capable of supporting human flourishing in a post-labor economy — but the path, the starting configuration, and the GPS categories that most urgently need attention at each stage of each form's development.

Chapter 24

Five archetypes — institution types and where to start

How the sixteen GPS categories assemble for the community land trust, the worker cooperative, the mutual aid network, the community foundation, and the civic technology cooperative — and what each form needs most to develop

Synthesis Applied framework Five institutional forms All 16 GPS categories

The GPS framework is form-agnostic. A community land trust and a worker cooperative both need governance, human development processes, financial stewardship, and accountability architecture. But they need them in different configurations, and the categories that are most urgent at founding differ significantly across institutional forms. This chapter translates the GPS framework into five specific institutional contexts — not to produce five separate frameworks, but to show practitioners what the shared framework looks like from inside their particular institutional form.

The five archetypes that follow are not exhaustive. The solidarity economy encompasses dozens of institutional forms — housing cooperatives, credit unions, time banks, community health centers, solidarity agricultural cooperatives, neighborhood development organizations, disability-led mutual aid collectives, and more — each with its own GPS profile. The five selected represent the forms most commonly being built or rebuilt in the contexts where post-labor economic transition is most acutely felt: the community land trust for the housing crisis, the worker cooperative for the employment transition, the mutual aid network for the immediate welfare gap, the community foundation for the capital mobilization challenge, and the civic technology cooperative for the digital infrastructure of post-labor community life. Together they illustrate how GPS assessment translates into institution-specific development priorities, which is the core practical skill this chapter is designed to build.

Each archetype below covers: what the institution is and how it governs; which GPS categories are most critical for this form and why; what the typical maturity profile looks like at founding; where the characteristic vulnerabilities are; and a realistic first-year development sequence. The chapter closes with a cross-archetype comparison.

01 ◈

Community land trust

Land & housing
What it is and how it governs

A community land trust (CLT) is a nonprofit organization that acquires land and holds it permanently in community ownership, making the improvements on that land — homes, commercial spaces, community facilities — available to residents and users at permanently affordable costs. The CLT separates land ownership (retained by the trust in perpetuity) from building ownership or occupancy rights (held by individuals or community businesses under long-term ground leases that include resale restrictions preserving affordability for future residents). This structure removes land from the speculative market, preventing the displacement that accompanies rising land values when housing is held as conventional private property.

CLT governance is characteristically tripartite: the governing board includes equal representation from current residents (those living in CLT homes), the broader community (neighbors and stakeholders who are not CLT residents), and public interest representatives (government officials, housing advocates, lenders). This governance structure reflects the CLT's core design principle — that the land trust exists to serve not only current residents but the community as a whole across generations — by balancing resident voice with broader community accountability. The tripartite board is one of the strongest governance designs in the solidarity economy for resisting the exclusivity that affects resident-only cooperative governance, and one of the most demanding to sustain because it requires maintaining authentic participation across three distinct constituency groups simultaneously.

Where to start — five GPS development priorities for a new CLT
01
Build GPS 10.0 asset governance before the first acquisition
The ground lease is the CLT's foundational legal document — it must be designed before the first home is conveyed, not adapted after. Develop a standard ground lease with community input on the resale formula, occupancy requirements, and stewardship obligations. Document the stewardship processes (annual inspections, resale protocols, default management) before there are residents who depend on them. Asset governance that is built prospectively produces more equitable implementation than asset governance built reactively when the first dispute arises.
02
Design tripartite governance for all three constituencies, not just the most accessible
New CLTs routinely fill their boards with housing advocates, lenders, and public officials (the easiest constituencies to recruit) while leaving resident and community member seats empty or filled with proxies. The governance is formally tripartite and substantively captured. Design recruitment processes, term structures, and board preparation for the most difficult constituencies first. The community member third is the most commonly neglected and the most important for the CLT's democratic legitimacy.
03
Develop GPS 3.0 access infrastructure before marketing to residents
The CLT model is counterintuitive to prospective residents who have been taught that homeownership means full ownership. Access and engagement processes must explain the model clearly, address the most common concerns (can I sell? can I benefit from appreciation? what do I actually own?), and support prospective residents through the ground lease review and closing process. Access barriers that are not designed away produce lower demand from more marginalized residents — exactly the demographic the CLT is designed to serve.
04
Build GPS 9.0 revenue diversification from founding
CLTs with single-source capital funding are vulnerable to government priority changes. From the first operational year, develop at least three revenue sources: public subsidy (land acquisitions, affordable housing programs), earned revenue (stewardship fees, ground lease payments), and philanthropic investment. The stewardship fee — a modest annual fee from residents that covers the CLT's ongoing stewardship of their occupancy — is both a revenue source and a demonstration that the CLT relationship is mutual rather than charitable.
05
Connect to the national CLT network before the first acquisition
The Community Land Trust Network holds the accumulated practice knowledge of three decades of CLT development — standard ground lease templates, stewardship protocols, acquisition financing models, governance curricula, and peer practitioners who have solved the problems a new CLT will encounter. GPS 12.0 inter-institutional relations investment pays higher returns earlier for CLTs than for almost any other institutional form because the network is exceptionally well-developed and accessible.
Post-labor transition fit

The CLT is the most directly relevant institutional form for the housing dimension of post-labor economic transition. As labor market income becomes more volatile and less reliably sufficient for conventional homeownership, the CLT's permanently affordable housing model provides the residential stability that makes all other flourishing dimensions more achievable. The CLT also embodies the commons stewardship logic that is central to post-labor institutional design: land held as a community asset rather than a speculative commodity is the institutional operationalization of the commons principle applied to the foundational resource — shelter — that all other human flourishing depends on.

Worker cooperative

Democratic enterprise
What it is and how it governs

A worker cooperative is a business owned and governed by its workers — each worker-member holds an ownership stake, participates in governance through democratic decision-making processes, and shares in the business's financial results through patronage distributions. Worker cooperatives are the most developed institutional form for democratizing the employment relationship: they retain the productive structure of a business (generating revenue by providing goods or services) while replacing the capital-over-labor governance of conventional firms with worker-majority governance that gives those who do the work authority over how the work is organized, compensated, and directed.

Worker cooperative governance centers on the general assembly of worker-members, which holds ultimate authority over major decisions, and on an elected board of directors that governs between assemblies. Management roles — the equivalent of a CEO and management team in a conventional firm — are accountable to the board rather than to external investors, which changes the character of management authority: managers in worker cooperatives serve at the pleasure of worker-members rather than at the pleasure of capital owners. This governance inversion is both the worker cooperative's most distinctive feature and its greatest organizational challenge: it requires worker-members to develop governance capacity that conventional employment does not provide, and it creates tensions between democratic governance processes and the operational decisiveness that competitive markets often require.

Characteristic strengths and structural vulnerabilities
Structural strengths
Democratic governance is structurally embedded — worker-members have formal authority over the enterprise that cannot be removed without dismantling the cooperative structure
Compensation equity is a structural governance priority — worker-members vote on pay ratios, making extreme compensation inequality structurally harder to sustain
Wealth distribution through capital accounts operationalizes shared ownership across all worker-members rather than concentrating returns in equity-holding leadership
Worker agency over work conditions is the most direct available response to the alienation of conventional employment that post-labor critique identifies
Structural vulnerabilities
Capitalization is structurally difficult — investor-equity capital is hostile to worker governance, and worker capital accumulation is slow; most new cooperatives are undercapitalized at founding (GPS 9.0)
Governance capacity gap — conventional employment does not develop governance skills; new worker-members who have never governed anything must quickly become effective governors
Democracy-efficiency tension is chronic — genuine democratic deliberation takes time that competitive market contexts do not consistently provide
Isomorphic pressure toward conventional firm practices — competitive market exposure gradually erodes cooperative governance distinctiveness if governance culture is not actively maintained
Where to start — five GPS development priorities for a new worker cooperative
01
GPS 7.0 first: invest in governance literacy before the first general assembly
Worker-members who do not understand cooperative governance — financial literacy, decision-making processes, rights and responsibilities of membership — cannot exercise their governance authority effectively. The governance literacy gap at founding produces the pattern where founding members with the most organizational experience dominate governance and newer members disengage. Design a cooperative onboarding curriculum that covers governance, financials, and member rights. Make it mandatory for membership, not optional for the engaged. The investment in governance education pays returns in every subsequent governance interaction.
02
Design the capital account structure before the first member joins
The internal capital account system — how membership stakes are priced, how patronage distributions accumulate, how departing members are bought out — is the most legally and financially consequential governance decision a worker cooperative makes. Getting it wrong at founding is expensive to correct. The Mondragon model's internal capital account structure, which retains most patronage distributions as internal capital rather than distributing them as cash, is one design precedent; many variations exist. Design with a cooperative attorney and a peer cooperative's financial officer before the first member is admitted.
03
Establish pay ratio governance in the founding governance documents
The compensation ratio between the lowest-paid and highest-paid worker-members is one of the most consequential equity decisions the cooperative makes and one of the most politically difficult to address after founding norms are established. Setting a ratio — many cooperatives use 3:1 or 4:1, Mondragon historically used 6:1 — in the founding documents creates structural accountability for the equity commitment that the cooperative's democratic governance aspires to. Without it, market pressures gradually push compensation structures toward conventional firm patterns.
04
Build a peer cooperative relationship before the first year is complete
The learning curve for worker cooperative governance, financial management, and democratic decision-making is steep and well-documented. Peer cooperatives who have already navigated the founding governance challenges — bylaw design, first conflict resolution, first board election, first patronage distribution — provide the most relevant and accessible knowledge. The USFWC's Democracy at Work Institute and regional cooperative development centers maintain these connections. A peer mentorship relationship with an established cooperative in a related sector is worth more than most consulting investments at the founding stage.
05
Design for democratic succession from founding
Worker cooperatives that do not design for leadership succession typically become founder-dependent: the founding member(s) who know the most become indispensable, and their departure triggers governance crises. Design the board election and management succession processes before they are needed. Develop the next generation of governance leaders from founding. Cross-train across governance roles so no single member holds irreplaceable institutional knowledge. Succession design is GPS 13.5 work; it should begin in Year 1, not Year 7 when the first founder departure becomes imminent.
Post-labor transition fit

The worker cooperative is the most direct institutional response to the governance dimension of post-labor transition: the collapse of the labor-value contract as the organizing principle of economic life makes worker ownership of productive enterprises the most available alternative to conventional employment for communities seeking economic self-determination. Cooperatives do not eliminate the work requirement — someone must do the work — but they transform the governance relationship around work from one in which capital directs labor to one in which workers direct themselves. In communities experiencing displacement, automation-driven unemployment, and labor market exclusion, the worker cooperative creates economic activity organized by and for the workers who perform it.

Mutual aid network

Solidarity & care
What it is and how it governs

A mutual aid network is a community-organized system of non-hierarchical reciprocal support — an infrastructure through which community members help each other meet needs that neither markets nor governments reliably address, without the power differentials that characterize charitable giving. The defining feature of mutual aid is its reciprocity: participants both give and receive, the relationship between helper and helped is not fixed, and the network does not require institutional approval or professional qualification to participate. Mutual aid networks are the oldest form of human solidarity, and their contemporary revival — accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic and by growing awareness of the systemic gaps in formal safety nets — represents one of the most significant institutional developments in the post-labor solidarity economy.

Mutual aid networks are deliberately anti-institutional in orientation: they resist formal governance structures, professional hierarchies, and the institutionalization processes that can transform solidarity into service delivery. This orientation is a strength (it produces the accessibility, speed, and community rootedness that formal institutions struggle to achieve) and a structural challenge for GPS assessment (the framework was designed for institutionalized processes, and many mutual aid practices intentionally resist institutionalization). The GPS categories most relevant to mutual aid networks are those that support the network's core solidarity function without bureaucratizing the community relationships that make that function work.

Characteristic strengths and structural vulnerabilities
Structural strengths
Accessibility is structurally prioritized — no professional gatekeeping, no eligibility screening, no institutional approval required to participate or receive support
Trust is built through relationship rather than through institutional authority — community members help people they know, creating bonds that formal institutions cannot replicate
Speed and flexibility exceeds formal institutions — network activation during crises can occur in hours rather than the days or weeks that institutional responses require
Dignity of help is preserved — receiving mutual aid does not carry the stigma of receiving charity, because the relationship is explicitly reciprocal
Structural vulnerabilities
Volunteer burnout is endemic — mutual aid networks without care infrastructure for their own participants routinely exhaust their most committed members during sustained crises (GPS 7.0 absent)
Resource sustainability is fragile — networks dependent on volunteer labor and individual donations have no reliable revenue base when crisis subsides and urgency recedes
Coordination capacity degrades with scale — the informal coordination that works for 20 people breaks down at 200; GPS 8.0 technology infrastructure is almost always behind the network's growth
Equity in distribution is harder to sustain informally — relationship-based distribution tends to favor those in existing social networks, reproducing the exclusions mutual aid is designed to address
Where to start — five GPS development priorities for a growing mutual aid network
01
Build GPS 7.0 care infrastructure for your own participants before the next surge
The most common mutual aid network failure mode is burning out the people who make the network work. Design care systems for network participants — peer support, workload sharing, emotional support for those doing frontline crisis care — before the next crisis creates the conditions that make burnout most likely. The mutual aid network that cannot take care of its own participants cannot sustain the care it provides to others. This is not a luxury; it is operational resilience infrastructure.
02
Implement GPS 8.0 coordination technology before the informal coordination breaks
Mutual aid networks almost universally use whatever communication tools their founding participants already use — group texts, Facebook groups, WhatsApp threads — which works at small scale and degrades rapidly with growth. Design the coordination infrastructure before the network grows past the point where informal tools work: need intake systems, resource matching tools, volunteer coordination platforms, and the data practices that protect participant privacy. Airtable, Signal, and purpose-built mutual aid platforms (Mutual Aid Hub, Distribute Aid) provide accessible options. Implement them when the network is small enough that transition is manageable.
03
Audit GPS 3.0 access equity before the next recruitment cycle
Who is and is not in the network's reach is determined by who is in the founding participants' social networks — which reflects existing social segregation rather than community need. Map who the network is currently reaching and who it is not. Design outreach processes that specifically recruit participants from communities not represented in the network's current membership. Equity in mutual aid distribution requires equity in network composition; relationship-based distribution reproduces existing social networks unless those networks are intentionally diversified.
04
Develop GPS 12.0 peer network and resource sharing relationships for resilience
Mutual aid networks that operate in isolation lose participants to burnout and resource gaps that peer networks could help address. Build relationships with two or three peer networks — for referrals when needs exceed the network's capacity, for resource sharing during surges, and for the peer support that prevents organizer isolation. The mutual aid network's core operating principle — solidarity is reciprocal — applies to its peer relationships as much as to its community relationships.
05
Make one deliberate GPS 1.0 governance decision about institutionalization
Every mutual aid network faces the institutionalization question: whether to remain an informal network, incorporate as a nonprofit, become a fiscal sponsorship project, or develop some hybrid structure. There is no universally correct answer — the decision should reflect the network's specific scale, sustainability needs, and governance values. The mistake is not deciding: networks that drift into institutionalization without deliberate governance choice often end up with the downsides of both formal and informal structure. Make the governance decision explicitly, with network participant input, and document the reasoning.
Post-labor transition fit

Mutual aid networks are the most immediate institutional response to the welfare gap created by post-labor economic transition: the period between when formal employment as the primary welfare mechanism weakens and when new institutional alternatives are mature enough to substitute for it. They fill the gap with directness, speed, and dignity that formal institutions cannot match. They are also the institutional form most specifically designed for the social connection dimension of flourishing: mutual aid creates the relationships, the reciprocity, and the sense of community membership that are among the most important non-material determinants of wellbeing. The network that forms to deliver food during a crisis often produces community bonds that outlast the crisis and become the social infrastructure of a more resilient community.

Community foundation

Capital & philanthropy
What it is and how it governs

A community foundation is a public charity that pools philanthropic capital from multiple donors and deploys it through grants to community organizations addressing local needs. Community foundations occupy a distinctive position in the solidarity economy: they hold and deploy capital at a scale that few individual solidarity economy institutions can access, and they have the potential to redirect that capital toward post-labor institutional development — seeding worker cooperatives, funding CLT land acquisitions, building mutual aid network capacity, and supporting the civic technology infrastructure of post-labor community life. That potential is unevenly realized: many community foundations remain primarily grant-making institutions aligned with conventional nonprofit models and donor preferences, and relatively few have made explicit commitment to the post-labor institutional development agenda that their capital position makes them particularly suited to advance.

Community foundation governance is typically board-driven, with boards composed of community leaders, major donors, and representatives of community constituencies. The governance tension in community foundations is the relationship between donor preferences (which determine much of the capital flow) and community needs (which should determine grantmaking priorities). Donor-advised funds — the fastest-growing asset type in community foundations — create specific accountability challenges: donors retain advisory rights over fund deployment, which can direct capital toward donor preferences rather than toward community priorities. GPS 9.0 and GPS 11.0 together describe the accountability architecture through which community foundations can navigate this tension without surrendering to donor capture.

Structural strengths
Capital scale exceeds any individual solidarity economy institution — the capital mobilization potential of community foundations is the largest available lever for post-labor institutional development
Financial management maturity is typically the highest GPS score at founding — accounting, investment management, and grant administration processes are generally well-developed

Where to start — five GPS development priorities for a community foundation committing to post-labor institutional development
01
Conduct a GPS 11.0 equity audit of grantmaking patterns
Apply the six-domain equity audit framework to the foundation's own grantmaking: who is receiving grants, in what amounts, for what purposes, and how does that distribution compare to the demographic profile of community need? Most community foundations discover significant disparities — toward larger, more established organizations; toward communities with more institutional infrastructure; away from the communities experiencing the most acute post-labor economic displacement. The audit findings should drive grantmaking strategy revision, not defensive interpretation.
02
Design GPS 16.0 community voice into grantmaking governance
The community foundation's grantmaking decisions — which organizations to fund, at what levels, for what purposes — are among the most consequential institutional decisions affecting the community it claims to serve. Yet the community that is most affected has typically the least formal influence over those decisions. Design at least one grantmaking program with genuine community decision authority: participatory grantmaking in which community members (not staff or donors) make final funding decisions. Several community foundations have implemented this model; the design precedents are available.
03
Deploy GPS 10.0 capital toward post-labor institutional development explicitly
Most community foundations lack an explicit strategy for funding the development of new institutional forms — cooperatives, CLTs, mutual aid infrastructure — as distinct from funding existing organizations doing established work. Develop a capital deployment strategy that explicitly targets post-labor institutional development: seed grants for worker cooperative conversions, land acquisition capital for community land trusts, infrastructure funding for mutual aid networks, development funding for civic technology cooperatives. RSF Social Finance's integrated capital model is a practical precedent for how philanthropic capital can support the full capital stack of post-labor institutions.
04
Restructure donor-advised fund governance to reduce donor capture
Donor-advised funds that give donors effective veto authority over capital deployment toward community priorities reproduce donor preferences rather than community needs. Redesign DAF governance to include community accountability mechanisms: community review of DAF deployment patterns, floor commitments for community-directed grantmaking from DAF assets, and donor education about the post-labor institutional development priorities that the foundation has adopted. Donors who are unwilling to accept community accountability for their philanthropy are not well-aligned with a community foundation's public purpose.
05
Invest in GPS 12.0 relationships with post-labor institutions, not only with grantees
The community foundation's most valuable contribution to the post-labor solidarity economy is often not its grantmaking but its convening capacity and its relationships. Build sustained, peer relationships with the worker cooperatives, CLTs, mutual aid networks, and civic technology cooperatives in the community — not as grantees to be managed but as institutional partners whose knowledge and perspective should shape the foundation's own strategy. The foundation that sees the solidarity economy only through the lens of its grantmaking relationship will systematically misunderstand what the community needs its capital to do.
Post-labor transition fit

Community foundations occupy a pivotal position in post-labor institutional development: they are the primary existing vehicle for mobilizing private philanthropic capital toward community benefit at scale. Their post-labor transition contribution depends on whether they direct that capital toward the institutional forms — cooperatives, CLTs, mutual aid networks, civic technology — that build community economic self-determination, or whether they continue directing it toward the charitable service delivery models that address symptoms of post-labor displacement without building the institutional alternatives to it. The foundation that chooses the former becomes the capital infrastructure of post-labor transition; the foundation that chooses the latter funds the cleanup operation of a transition it is not accelerating.

Civic technology cooperative

Digital infrastructure
What it is and how it governs

A civic technology cooperative is a worker-owned technology organization that builds and maintains digital infrastructure — platforms, tools, data systems, applications — specifically for community benefit rather than for commercial scale. It combines the worker ownership and democratic governance of a worker cooperative with a mission orientation toward the digital public goods that post-labor communities need: accessible civic participation platforms, community resource coordination tools, open-source alternatives to commercially extractive platforms, and the data sovereignty infrastructure that allows communities to control information about their own members rather than ceding it to corporate platforms.

Civic technology cooperatives are simultaneously worker cooperatives (governed by worker-member democracy, facing all the governance and capitalization challenges of that form) and technology organizations (facing the specific product development, technical staffing, and sustainability challenges of software-based businesses). Their GPS profile therefore overlaps significantly with the worker cooperative archetype — particularly in GPS 7.0, 1.0, and 9.0 — while adding the specific technology infrastructure obligations of GPS 8.0 and the community accountability obligations that civic mission requires in GPS 11.0, 14.0, and 15.0. They are among the most complex institutional forms to develop because they must build governance maturity, technical capability, and community accountability simultaneously.

Characteristic strengths and structural vulnerabilities
Structural strengths
Technology products can scale without proportional staffing — well-designed civic technology serves exponentially more users than the worker-members who built it, multiplying community benefit
Open-source development creates knowledge commons — civic technology cooperatives that open-source their work contribute permanent digital public goods to the solidarity economy
Worker-owner technical expertise creates rare alignment between democratic governance and operational excellence — the workers who govern are also the workers with the deepest understanding of what the institution is building
Data sovereignty orientation distinguishes civic technology from commercial alternatives — community control of community data is both an ethical commitment and a market differentiator in an era of platform surveillance capitalism
Structural vulnerabilities
Technical talent retention — compensation competition from commercial technology firms creates chronic staffing pressure; worker cooperative pay equity commitments are hardest to sustain in high-demand technical labor markets
Revenue model fragility — civic technology often cannot charge the prices that commercial technology charges, creating chronic underfunding of the infrastructure that community benefit requires
Technical debt accumulation — GPS 8.0 technology infrastructure for the institution's own operations is often neglected while technical capacity is directed toward community-facing products
Community accountability gap — technical expertise creates internal authority asymmetries where non-technical community stakeholders cannot meaningfully assess the governance of technical systems affecting them (GPS 14.0)
Where to start — five GPS development priorities for a new civic technology cooperative
01
GPS 2.0: design the digital public goods framework before the first product launch
The civic technology cooperative builds public goods — digital infrastructure intended to serve community needs rather than extract commercial value. Design the public goods framework explicitly: what does it mean for this software to be a public good? What license governs it? Who can use, modify, and redistribute it? What community accountability governs its development? The open-source license decision (which open-source license, and why) is the GPS 2.0 foundational process for a civic technology cooperative, and it should be made deliberately rather than by default.
02
GPS 14.0: establish AI governance before any AI feature ships
Civic technology cooperatives building tools used by post-labor communities will encounter decisions about AI feature integration — automated recommendations, algorithmic prioritization, machine learning classification — that have governance implications for the communities using the tools. Establish the AI governance framework before the first AI feature ships: what risk tier applies to each use case, what community transparency is required, what oversight mechanisms exist, and what equity monitoring is in place. Technology cooperatives that ship AI features without governance infrastructure are replicating the same governance failures they may be building alternatives to.
03
GPS 9.0: design the revenue model for long-term sustainability, not just launch viability
Civic technology cooperatives frequently launch with grant funding, develop a user base, and then face a sustainability cliff when grant funding ends and the user base is unwilling to pay commercial prices for tools they have been receiving for free. Design the revenue model at founding with sustainability as the primary constraint: what combination of subscription fees, grant funding, government contracts, and solidarity pricing can sustain the product over five years? The revenue model must be designed for sustainability at the scale of community benefit the product is designed to produce — not for the initial launch scale.
04
GPS 16.0: build community governance of product decisions, not just feedback processes
Civic technology cooperatives typically design community feedback processes — user research, advisory committees, beta testing — that inform product decisions made by worker-members. Genuine community governance of civic technology requires more: decision rights for the communities the technology serves over the features, priorities, and terms of the product that serves them. Design at least one product decision domain — a roadmap priority vote, a data governance decision, a platform policy — in which community users have genuine decision authority rather than advisory input.
05
GPS 7.0: design governance literacy development for non-technical worker-members
Worker cooperatives with high internal expertise asymmetry — where some worker-members have technical knowledge that others do not — face governance challenges specific to that asymmetry: non-technical members defer to technical members on product decisions, creating a de facto technical oligarchy within formally democratic governance. Design governance literacy development that builds all worker-members' capacity to meaningfully participate in technical product decisions, and design product decision processes that make technical expertise accessible rather than gatekept. The democratic character of the cooperative depends on the accessibility of its governance, including its technical governance.
Post-labor transition fit

The civic technology cooperative addresses the digital infrastructure dimension of post-labor transition: the need for community-controlled digital tools for coordination, resource distribution, participatory governance, and economic activity that do not extract data and rent from the communities using them. As more community life moves to digital platforms, the governance of those platforms becomes a governance question for post-labor communities — and the civic technology cooperative is the institutional form designed to give communities democratic control over the digital infrastructure of their own lives. The alternative — commercial platforms whose governance is accountable to investors rather than to users — is the digital equivalent of the labor-capital governance inversion that post-labor institutions were designed to correct.

The framework is the same for all of them. The path through it is different for each. Knowing which archetype you are — and which GPS categories are most urgent given that form — is the beginning of institutional development that actually develops something.

On using the archetype profiles in practice: The five profiles in this chapter are starting points, not prescriptions. Real institutions rarely match a single archetype perfectly — a worker cooperative that owns its building will have a more demanding GPS 10.0 profile than the typical cooperative; a mutual aid network that has incorporated and built staff capacity will have a more demanding GPS 1.0 profile than the typical network. Use the archetype profiles as the closest available approximation, then adjust based on the specific features of the institution being assessed. The GPS maturity assessment described in Chapter 7 is the tool for the adjustment: it produces an institution-specific profile that supplements and refines the archetype baseline.

On sequencing across archetypes in a community: A community building post-labor institutional infrastructure is not building one archetype — it is building an ecosystem of complementary forms. The mutual aid network addresses immediate welfare gaps while the worker cooperative develops employment alternatives while the CLT builds permanent housing stability while the civic technology cooperative builds the digital infrastructure that all of them share. These institutions are not in competition; they are complementary components of a community's post-labor institutional ecosystem. The community foundation, when oriented toward post-labor institutional development, can be the capital infrastructure that supports all of them. GPS Category 12.0 (Inter-Institutional Relations) is the process domain through which these institutions build the relationships, knowledge-sharing, and mutual aid that make the ecosystem more than the sum of its parts.

On institutional forms not covered here: The five archetypes in this chapter are not exhaustive. Credit unions and community development financial institutions (CDFIs) address the financial infrastructure dimension of post-labor transition — building community-controlled capital access for communities excluded from conventional banking. Community health centers and disability-led care cooperatives address health and care in ways that neither market insurance nor government programs adequately cover. Time banks and solidarity economy currencies build alternative exchange infrastructure for non-market economic activity. Housing cooperatives address the same affordability challenge as CLTs through collective ownership rather than permanent affordability mechanisms. Each of these forms has its own GPS profile; the archetype analysis method demonstrated in this chapter applies to all of them. The five chosen here are starting points, not boundaries.
